# ❤️ Emotional

> *Resistance is information. It always has a source.*

---

## What this category is

Emotional intelligence tools apply structured reasoning to the human dimension of any situation — what people actually want (not what they say they want), why they're resisting, what's building or eroding trust, and what genuinely motivates their behavior. These tools don't deal in sentiment; they deal in the real incentive structures, fears, and identity narratives that shape human action.

The core premise: behavior that looks irrational usually isn't. It looks irrational because you're missing information about what that person is optimizing for. These tools surface that information.

---

## When to reach for it

- People are not doing what they agreed to do, and you don't understand why
- A proposal keeps hitting resistance that feels personal or political
- A negotiation, alignment effort, or change initiative isn't moving
- Trust has broken down in a relationship or team
- You're designing something for users and want to understand what they actually care about
- Someone's behavior seems misaligned with their stated goals

---

## When *not* to reach for it

**Don't use emotional tools as a substitute for direct conversation.** These tools help you prepare for and interpret human situations — they don't replace talking to people. When you can have the direct conversation, have it.

**Don't confuse emotional intelligence with manipulation.** Understanding someone's real motivations should inform how you serve them, not how you exploit them. Use the [ethics](../ethics/) toolkit if you're in territory where that line matters.

The nearest neighbor: [Social](../social/) — which maps organizational power, group dynamics, and incentive structures. Emotional tools focus on individuals and relationships; social tools focus on groups, organizations, and power.
