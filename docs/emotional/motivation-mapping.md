# emotional-motivation-mapping

> Maps what genuinely drives someone — beyond stated reasons and job descriptions.

**Trigger:** `/s4h-emotional-motivation-mapping`

---

## What it does

Builds a real motivation model for a person or group: their actual goals, fears, identity narrative, and how a situation activates or threatens each. The gap between stated motivation and real motivation is where behavior becomes hard to predict.

---

## When to use

- "Why are they behaving like this?"
- Incentives seem misaligned
- Need to understand someone to work with them, influence them, or design for them

---

## Example

**Input:**
> My VP of Engineering has been resistant to adopting any of the AI coding tools I'm proposing. He's technically capable, open to new technology in other domains, and says the right things in meetings. But nothing moves. Map his motivation.

**Output:**

**Stated motivation:** Supportive of AI tools in principle. No articulated objections.

**The gap:** Technically capable, open elsewhere, says yes in meetings — but nothing moves. This pattern suggests the resistance is not at the level of stated opinion. It's operating somewhere else.

**Possible real motivations at work:**

**Identity threat:** A VP of Engineering who built their credibility over 10 years on deep technical expertise may experience AI coding tools as a devaluation signal. If AI writes the code, what is the expert for? Even if they'd never say this, a threat to professional identity creates avoidance behavior that looks like bureaucratic delay.

**Team protection:** He may be protecting his team from the disruption cost of adopting a new tool during a period of delivery pressure. His actual motivation may be "don't slow my team down right now" — a reasonable motivation that's easier to express as general support without specific urgency.

**Accountability avoidance:** If he approves AI tools and something goes wrong (a security incident, a quality regression, hallucinated code in production), he owns that decision. Passive non-adoption has no accountability. This isn't cynical — it's rational risk management.

**Competence uncertainty:** He may not have personally used these tools and doesn't want to sponsor something he hasn't evaluated. Recommending tools he hasn't vetted feels risky. Avoidance is the safe behavior.

**What this suggests:**

The solution to each of these motivations is different:
- Identity threat → involve him in being the AI expert, not the person who adopted AI
- Team protection → propose a limited pilot with minimal disruption cost
- Accountability avoidance → share the accountability explicitly; propose a joint evaluation
- Competence uncertainty → give him a hands-on evaluation session before any proposal

The most likely combination is identity + accountability. A 1:1 conversation that says "I want your technical judgment on whether this is right for us" addresses both — it repositions him as the evaluator, not the adopter.
