# emotional-resistance-diagnosis

> Diagnoses the source of resistance — not just that it exists, but what's underneath it.

**Trigger:** `/s4h-emotional-resistance-diagnosis`

---

## What it does

Classifies resistance into its real type (misunderstanding, disagreement, fear, or values conflict) and identifies the specific source. Each type requires a completely different response — treating resistance as obstruction without diagnosing its source makes it worse.

---

## When to use

- "Why won't they get on board?"
- An initiative keeps hitting friction despite logical arguments
- A specific person or group is consistently resistant
- Pushback that feels emotional or personal rather than logical

---

## Example

**Input:**
> We've been trying to get the sales team to adopt our new CRM for 3 months. Leadership supports it. The product is genuinely better than the old one. But adoption is stuck at 40%. There's low-level grumbling but no one says anything specific. What's the resistance?

**Output:**

**Resistance type assessment:**

This pattern — low adoption, vague grumbling, no specific stated objections, leadership support, genuinely better product — is classic **fear resistance**, not disagreement resistance.

If it were disagreement resistance, you'd hear specific objections ("the pipeline view is worse," "it doesn't integrate with X"). The absence of specific articulated objections despite obvious friction is a fear signal.

**The likely fear sources:**

*Fear 1 — The CRM reveals what's not happening.* The old system may have had gaps, incomplete records, or informal workarounds that were invisible. The new system may expose those gaps to management. Reps who have messy pipelines, stale deals, or informal processes that don't show up in the old system have a rational fear that better tooling will make those things visible.

*Fear 2 — Learning curve = short-term performance dip.* Sales reps are measured on output. Learning a new tool means short-term efficiency loss. If there's no coverage for that dip (relief on quota during transition, for example), the rational move is minimal adoption.

*Fear 3 — This is the prelude to something else.* CRM adoption often precedes closer monitoring of activity metrics, funnel hygiene requirements, or process changes. The resistance may not be to the tool itself but to what it signals is coming.

**Why the grumbling is vague:**
None of these fears are safe to articulate publicly. "This will expose my messy pipeline to my manager" is not something a rep says in a team meeting. Vague grumbling is how unexpressible fear manifests.

**Response approach for fear resistance:**

1. Create a private channel for reps to voice concerns without attribution
2. Explicitly address what management will and won't be doing with CRM data
3. Provide quota relief or ramp period during adoption — remove the performance cost of the learning curve
4. Make it visible that the goal is to help reps close more, not to monitor them more closely

The key insight: arguing for the product's quality doesn't address any of these fears. The resistance isn't about the CRM's features.
