# 📜 Historical

> *Most failures have happened before. Most cycles have been named. The pattern is already there.*

---

## What this category is

Historical tools apply the record of what has actually happened to current decisions and situations. They find genuine precedents (as opposed to superficial analogies), extract the transferable principle from a specific case, identify what recurring cycle a situation is an instance of, and surface the failure modes that appear repeatedly across similar situations.

The discipline these tools enforce: distinguish between the contingent surface details of a historical case (specific to its time and place) and the underlying structural pattern that recurs. The surface details don't transfer. The structure does.

---

## When to reach for it

- Making a decision that feels like it has been made before — by someone, somewhere
- About to do something and want to know how it typically fails
- A situation feels familiar in an ominous way — want to know what pattern this is
- Have a historical case (a company failure, a market event, a policy outcome) and want to know what it actually teaches
- In a cycle of some kind — market, hype, organizational — and want to know where in it you are

---

## When *not* to reach for it

**Don't use historical tools when the situation is genuinely novel.** Some situations don't have meaningful precedents. Forcing a historical frame onto a genuinely new situation produces false confidence. If you can't find a case that's structurally similar (not just superficially similar), the historical analysis may mislead.

**Don't mistake pattern recognition for prediction.** History rhymes; it doesn't repeat exactly. These tools improve judgment; they don't eliminate uncertainty.

The nearest neighbor: [Analogy](../analogy/) — which imports mechanisms from other *domains*, while historical tools find precedents from *past events*.
