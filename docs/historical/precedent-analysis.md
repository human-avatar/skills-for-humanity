# historical-precedent-analysis

> Finds genuinely similar historical situations to inform a current decision.

**Trigger:** `/historical-precedent-analysis`

---

## What it does

Identifies historical situations that are structurally similar to the current one (not just superficially), analyzes what decision-makers did and what happened, distinguishes the contingent details from the transferable lessons, and applies those lessons to the current situation.

---

## When to use

- "Has this happened before?"
- Making a decision that might repeat a known pattern
- "What does history say about this?"
- Want to learn from precedent before making a consequential choice

---

## Example

**Input:**
> Our company is considering going head-to-head with a much larger competitor that has just entered our market. We've been the leader in our niche for 4 years. They have 20x our resources. What do the historical precedents say?

**Output:**

**Candidate precedents:**

**Precedent 1: WordPerfect vs. Microsoft Word (1980s-90s)**
WordPerfect dominated word processing. Microsoft entered with Word, initially inferior, and won by integrating with Windows. *Structural similarity: established niche leader, larger entrant, platform integration as leverage. Lesson: the established player lost because it underestimated the entrant's willingness to sustain losses and leverage an adjacent advantage (the OS). The resource asymmetry matters less than the structural position.*

**Precedent 2: Craigslist vs. vertical specialists (2000s-2010s)**
Craigslist dominated classifieds. Vertical competitors (Airbnb, StubHub, Etsy) didn't go head-to-head — they focused on specific categories where depth of specialization mattered more than breadth. *Structural similarity: large generalist entering a niche market, established player. Lesson: depth beats breadth in specific categories. The vertical specialists won by being objectively better for a specific use case, not by competing on resources.*

**Precedent 3: Dropbox vs. Google Drive**
Dropbox was the category creator. Google entered with vastly more resources, bundled with Google Workspace. *Structural similarity: 4-year incumbent, resource-rich entrant. Lesson: Dropbox survived by focusing on the enterprise segment that valued simplicity and cross-platform functionality over Google's ecosystem lock-in. Niche focus within the broader category, not head-to-head competition, kept them viable.*

**Transferable lessons:**

1. **Going head-to-head on the large competitor's terms is almost always the wrong move.** In all three precedents, the smaller player that survived did so by redefining the competitive battlefield — deeper on a segment, better for a specific use case, or differentiated on a dimension the large entrant doesn't prioritize.

2. **The large entrant's advantage is resources and reach; your advantage is depth, speed, and specific-use-case fit.** Compete on depth.

3. **Expect a margin squeeze.** Large entrants often price aggressively to accelerate adoption. A 4-year niche leader typically cannot win a price war. Don't try.

4. **Find the segment within your niche where your advantage is most durable.** Who in your existing customer base would *never* switch to a large-company solution, even a free one? Build your defense around them.

**What this doesn't tell you:** Whether your specific market dynamics match these precedents closely enough for these lessons to apply. The structural similarity test matters — do your customers actually value depth over breadth? If your market treats your niche as commodity, the Dropbox lesson may not apply.
