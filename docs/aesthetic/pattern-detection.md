# aesthetic-pattern-detection

> Identifies the underlying formal pattern at work — because naming the pattern unlocks the playbook.

**Trigger:** `/s4h-aesthetic-pattern-detection`

---

## What it does

Identifies the deep structural patterns operating in any designed thing — presentation, argument, strategy, product, piece of writing. Works through seven formal patterns (repetition, symmetry, hierarchy, contrast, tension/resolution, figure/ground, part/whole), matches to cross-domain archetypes, and names the dominant pattern. The value: once the pattern is named, you can use its full playbook — including diagnosing what's violating it.

---

## When to use

- "What pattern is this?"
- "Why does this work — or not work?"
- "What structure is at play here?"
- Designing something and want to be deliberate about the formal structure
- Something is inconsistent and you can't name why

## When NOT to use

- When you want to simplify — use `aesthetic-simplicity-analysis`
- When you want to test internal consistency — use `aesthetic-coherence-check`
- When the question is structural similarity between two situations (not a single artefact) — use `analogy-structure-mapping`

---

## Example

**Input:**
> Analyze the structure of our quarterly business review format: we open with a company health dashboard (metrics), then go division by division with each division presenting in the same format (performance, challenges, asks), then close with a cross-functional themes slide and a CEO summary. It feels mechanical. What pattern is operating?

**Output:**

---

**Formal Patterns Present**

- **Repetition/Rhythm:** Strong — each division section follows the same three-part format (performance → challenges → asks). Creates predictability, which is useful for processing speed but produces monotony when nothing subverts the rhythm.
- **Hierarchy:** Present in structure (company level → division level → cross-functional) but not functioning — the company dashboard at the opening creates a top-level view but then the format descends into equal-weight division segments. There is no mechanism for attention to be directed toward what matters most.
- **Figure/Ground:** Absent — every division gets the same treatment. There is no figure (the most important thing) against ground (the supporting context). Everything is equally visible, which means nothing stands out.
- **Tension/Resolution:** Missing. The format presents problems (challenges) and requests (asks) but contains no mechanism for creating stakes, no movement toward resolution, no question that builds across the meeting.

**Archetype Match:** **Inventory report** — the form most resembles a stock-taking format where each unit reports its state. This is appropriate for an audit; it is not appropriate for a strategic decision-making session. The closest productive archetype the format is trying to be but isn't quite: **portfolio review** (McKinsey/VC format), which maintains repetition for comparability but introduces hierarchy through relative emphasis on the most important items.

**Pattern Name:** Flat inventory with repetitive structure — "the equal-weight report"

**What Naming It Reveals:**

The format is optimized for completeness (every division gets heard) rather than for impact (the most important things get the most attention). This is a political design pattern, not a strategic one — equal treatment prevents conflict about who matters more, but produces meetings where nothing feels urgent and decisions are diffuse. The mechanical feeling is structural: there is no figure/ground, no tension, and no asymmetry to create attention dynamics.

---

**Pattern Implications**

| Implication | Description |
|---|---|
| What should come next | The meeting needs a tension introduced early — a single question or problem that gives meaning to the data that follows. "What are we trying to decide today?" |
| What is violating the pattern | The CEO summary at the end is the only potential figure element, but it comes after 90 minutes of equal-weight material has flattened attention |
| Intentional or accidental | The equal-weight structure is almost certainly political (everyone gets equal time) not strategic — accidental incoherence, not productive subversion |

**Recommended Pattern Shift:**

Shift from inventory report to **tension-and-resolution arc**. Introduce one governing question at the start (the tension). Let division updates be read against that question, not as independent status reports. Use the CEO summary to resolve — not to restate — what was learned. This introduces figure/ground (the governing question is the figure; division data is the ground) and tension/resolution (the whole meeting has a destination). The repetition in division format can stay — it becomes a useful scaffold once hierarchy and tension are added.
