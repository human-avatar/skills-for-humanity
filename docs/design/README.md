# 🔄 Design

> *The best design is the one you cannot subtract from.*

---

## What this category is

Design thinking tools apply the discipline of form-giving to problems of fit — finding what people actually need, working productively within constraints, shaping through iteration, and cutting to the essential. They encode the methodology of the great practitioners: Dieter Rams' principle that good design is as little design as possible; Don Norman's insistence that designers are systematically wrong about users until they observe them; Christopher Alexander's understanding that every good form is a resolution of forces in tension; Jony Ive's discipline of starting from the hardest constraints and letting design decisions cascade from there.

The unifying principle is that design is discovered, not planned. Good form cannot be derived purely from reasoning about requirements. It must be tested against reality — real users, real constraints, real use — and refined until it fits. Each tool in this category structures one part of that process: understanding the real need before building the wrong thing; mapping constraints so you know what territory is actually open; iterating with discipline so each cycle produces learning rather than motion; removing what isn't necessary so the essential form can emerge.

Design problems are not solved by adding. The best designs achieve their function by finding the form that is complete and nothing more.

---

## When to reach for it

- You have a design brief and suspect the stated requirements aren't the real requirements
- A product, document, or system has grown beyond what it needs to be
- You need to prototype and test a design direction but don't know what to build or at what fidelity
- You're working within a complex set of constraints and need to understand what design space they leave
- Something exists that feels over-complicated — more should be removable but you're not sure what
- You're stuck choosing between design directions and need a structured way to test them
- The problem has been framed as a solution and you want to find the need underneath

---

## When *not* to reach for it

**Don't use design tools when the problem is primarily strategic, not form-giving.** If the question is which market to enter, which product to build, or what direction to bet on — that's a decision problem, not a design problem. Start with `/s4h-decision` to make the strategic call, then bring design tools to the execution of it.

**Design thinking is not the same as aesthetic refinement.** If something already works and the question is purely visual — does this look right, is the visual hierarchy correct, does it cohere — reach for the aesthetic toolkit. Design tools ask "does the form serve the function?"; aesthetic tools ask "does the form cohere as a visual whole?" The distinction matters because fixing the aesthetics of a design that hasn't found its function is expensive work that may need to be undone.

The nearest neighbor: [Aesthetic](../aesthetic/) — for visual coherence, pattern detection, and elegance analysis once the functional form is established.
