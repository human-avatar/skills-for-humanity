# `/strategy-positioning`

> *The good fighters of old first put themselves beyond the possibility of defeat, and then waited for an opportunity of defeating the enemy.*

**Category:** [Strategy](README.md) · **Command:** `/strategy-positioning` · **Source:** Sun Tzu, *The Art of War*; Michael Porter, competitive strategy theory

---

## What it does

Builds the conditions for competitive unassailability before the contest begins — identifying the ideal position to hold, the assets required to hold it, the gap between current and ideal, and the sequenced investments that widen the moat. The output is not a positioning statement; it is an honest assessment of what it would take to occupy ground a rational, well-resourced opponent would choose to go around.

The framework draws on Sun Tzu's principle that unassailability precedes opportunity, and Porter's insight that sustainable competitive advantage requires trade-offs — commitments that make you excellent in one direction at the cost of flexibility, and which cannot be replicated by competitors without abandoning their own positions.

---

## When to use it

- Tired of competing on every dimension against better-resourced opponents and want to find ground where the contest is won structurally
- Building a consulting or freelance practice and want to become the obvious choice in a specific niche rather than competing on price with generalists
- A product, team, or business is getting commoditized — customers are treating you as interchangeable and you want to change that
- About to make a significant investment and want to ensure it builds toward a position rather than just solving today's problem
- Evaluating whether current positioning is genuine or merely asserted — whether you actually hold what you claim to hold

---

## When *not* to use it

**Don't use positioning analysis if you haven't validated the market for the position.** Sun Tzu's unassailability principle requires that the position is one an opponent would want. Building an unassailable position in a segment no one cares about is perfectly executed work on the wrong problem. Terrain analysis tells you which positions exist and are valuable; positioning analysis tells you how to hold one once you've identified it.

**Don't mistake the analysis for the work.** Positioning is built through investment, relationship, reputation, and time. The nearest neighbor: `/strategy-terrain` — which identifies available positions before you decide which to build toward. Run terrain first when you're not yet certain which ground is worth holding.

---

## Full example

**Input:**
> I'm a freelance consultant — organizational design and leadership development. I've been at it for three years, have a solid client base, but I'm constantly competing on price with larger generalist firms and solo consultants who undercut me. I want to stop competing on price and become the go-to person for a specific type of work. How do I do that?

---

**Output:**

### Positioning Analysis

**Ideal Unassailable Position**

The position you're describing — "go-to person for a specific type of work" — requires being something that no competing option is: the first-call choice among a defined buyer community because you are recognized as having the most relevant, deepest, most demonstrated expertise for a specific problem.

Concretely, that looks like:

*"The consultant for organizational design at [specific company profile or transition type] — known by the buyers who matter, with visible case studies from recognized organizations, a track record with that exact problem type, and a reputation that means buyers tell each other about you rather than posting to LinkedIn."*

Specificity is the prerequisite. "Organizational design and leadership development" is a domain, not a position. The unassailable position requires picking a corner of that domain where you can be genuinely singular. Examples:
- Post-merger organizational integration for companies under $500M
- Leadership team effectiveness for PE-backed businesses in the first 12 months post-acquisition
- Organizational design for companies scaling from 50 to 200 people
- Building succession-ready leadership pipelines for family-owned businesses transitioning leadership

At that specificity, generalist firms are overqualified (too expensive and generic), other solo consultants are likely generalists (relevant experience spread thin), and you are the obvious match. A rational buyer looking at that problem wants the person who has done it ten times — not the person who has done adjacent things.

**Required Assets and Capabilities**

To hold that position, you need:

- *Three to five visible case studies* that specifically match the niche problem, from recognizable clients — visible means publicly citeable, not under NDA
- *A point of view* that is yours: a specific methodology, framework, or perspective on the niche problem that shows buyers you've thought about it more deeply than a generalist would
- *A referral network* within the buyer community for that niche — the three or four people in that world who, when asked "who should I call?", say your name
- *Presence in the channels where buyers look* — not general LinkedIn content, but the specific forums, publications, communities, or events where buyers for that niche problem actually gather
- *A track record of outcomes* — not "engagement completed" but "what changed, and how much did it matter"

*Currently have* (based on three years of work):
- A client base who have seen your work — but probably under NDAs that limit citability
- Domain knowledge across organizational design and leadership development
- Presumably some work that is closer to the niche than other work — projects you remember as being "in your zone"

*Currently missing:*
- Public case studies at the specificity of the niche position
- A declared point of view on a specific problem type
- Active presence in niche-specific channels (vs. general consulting communities)
- Likely: explicit referral infrastructure (people who recommend you are probably doing so informally, not systematically)

**Current Position**

Honest assessment: you hold a generalist position with a reputation for quality among clients who've worked with you, competing in a market where price is the primary differentiator because buyers cannot distinguish your quality from competitors' claims without having worked with you.

That's not a failure — it's the natural state before niche positioning is built. But it means your current position generates work through existing relationships and referrals from people who already know you, and it stalls when you try to acquire new clients who have no reason to pick you over anyone else.

**Gap-Widening Moves**

Ranked by leverage per unit of effort:

1. *Identify your three best past projects* — the ones where you did the most differentiated work, the client got the most value, and the problem type most closely matches the niche you want to own. These are your raw material for case studies. Start conversations with those clients now about what you can make public, even in anonymized form. Cost: moderate (relationship conversations). Leverage: high (without public evidence, the niche claim is asserted, not demonstrated).

2. *Write one substantive point-of-view piece* on the specific problem type you're claiming. Not a LinkedIn post — a framework, a guide, a detailed perspective that someone in the niche would find genuinely useful. This signals your methodology before a buyer calls you and gives you something to share in early conversations that does the positioning work without a sales pitch. Cost: 15–20 hours. Leverage: high (a well-written POV piece circulates within a niche community for years).

3. *Make one referral-infrastructure move* — identify the three or four people in your target buyer community who are connectors (trusted, asked for recommendations, active in the community). Invest specifically in those relationships. Have lunch, help them with something, share the POV piece. Cost: moderate (sustained relationship effort over 3–6 months). Leverage: very high — referrals from trusted connectors in a niche community are the dominant acquisition channel for specialist consultants.

4. *Be visible in the one channel where buyers for your niche actually gather* — a specific conference, online community, or publication. Show up consistently, contribute substantially, do not sell. Cost: moderate (time + possible membership). Leverage: medium-high over 12–18 months.

5. *Decline or redirect work that doesn't match the niche.* This is the hardest move and the one that distinguishes positioning from positioning theater. Every time you take a generalist project "because the money is there," you delay the niche case studies that would build the real position. Not refusing everything — but having a filter. Cost: short-term revenue reduction. Leverage: significant over 12–18 months as niche positioning compounds.

**Position Test Result**

Applying the test: would a rational, well-resourced competitor (a generalist firm with a bench of consultants) choose a different arena rather than compete with you on your niche position, once fully built?

For a large firm: yes. The economics of deploying their team on a highly specialized niche engagement don't make sense when their margin comes from larger, multi-workstream engagements. They would refer you rather than compete.

For another specialist solo consultant: maybe. If another solo builds the same niche position, you have a genuine competitor. The moat here is not structural (you cannot patent a niche); it is relational and reputational. Your moat is that you got there first, built the relationships, and have the visible track record. That is harder to replicate than it sounds — the referral network in a tight niche is deeply resistant to newcomers without the relationship infrastructure already in place.

**Implementation Path**

*Months 1–2:* Decide the specific niche. Write the POV piece. Have the case study conversations with three past clients. These are table-stakes moves — don't build the referral network before you know what you're known for.

*Months 2–6:* Identify the three connector relationships and invest in them. Show up in the niche's primary channel. Begin directing inbound toward niche work.

*Months 6–18:* Niche position begins to compound as referrals, the POV piece, and visible case studies create a self-reinforcing reputation. This is when you begin declining work that doesn't fit, because the niche work is sufficient.

*Year 2+:* The position is held. A rational competitor evaluating the niche sees your track record, referral network, and visible methodology and decides to find a different niche. You have built what Sun Tzu meant by being "beyond the possibility of defeat."
