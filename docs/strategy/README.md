# ⚔️ Strategy

> *Advantage is created before the contest begins.*

---

## What this category is

The strategy toolkit is an adversarial reasoning toolkit drawing on Sun Tzu (*The Art of War*), Clausewitz (*On War*), Miyamoto Musashi (*The Book of Five Rings*), and competitive theory. It is not about aggression — it is about reading competitive contexts clearly and acting with timing, economy, and intelligence.

The unifying principle: "The victorious warrior wins first and then goes to war, while the defeated warrior goes to war first and then seeks to win." Strategy is what happens before the contest. The skills in this category — terrain mapping, intelligence auditing, timing analysis, force economy, positioning, information management, victory definition, and alliance building — are the disciplines that create the conditions under which winning becomes likely before any direct contest begins.

---

## When to reach for it

- Making a competitive decision where position, timing, or opponent behavior matters
- Entering a negotiation and wanting to assess your leverage and information gaps honestly
- Outgunned or under-resourced and needing to find the minimum effective path to the objective
- Building toward a position you want to hold in a market, organization, or relationship
- Managing what parties know about you in a competitive context
- Not sure what winning actually looks like — and needing to define it before acting
- Building organizational or market coalitions where different parties have different interests

---

## When *not* to reach for it

**Don't use strategy skills for cooperative situations.** When you and other parties genuinely share objectives and the question is coordination — who does what, how to sequence decisions, how to allocate shared resources — strategy tools impose an adversarial framing that distorts the analysis. In cooperative contexts, decision tools and systems tools are more appropriate. The presence of competition doesn't automatically require strategy framing; sometimes the right move is to make the situation cooperative.

**Don't substitute strategy for decision-making.** The nearest neighbor trap: Decision tools (the decision category) help you choose between options based on criteria, values, and uncertainty. Strategy tools create the conditions that make one option preferable to the others — they operate upstream. If you're evaluating two clear options against known criteria, that's a decision problem. If you're figuring out how to change the terrain so that one option becomes obviously correct before others see it the same way, that's a strategy problem.
