# `/strategy-deception`

> *All warfare is based on deception. Hence, when able to attack, we must seem unable; when using our forces, we must seem inactive.*

**Category:** [Strategy](README.md) · **Command:** `/strategy-deception` · **Source:** Sun Tzu, *The Art of War*, Book I

---

## What it does

Analyzes what information to protect in a legitimate competitive context, what impressions work in your favor, what credible signals can create those impressions, and what your opponent may be concealing from you. The output is an information management plan — not a script for lying, but a disciplined audit of what to make visible, what to protect, and where inconsistencies would expose the whole approach.

The framework applies in commercial negotiation, competitive market positioning, organizational politics, and any context where managing what the other party believes about you is a standard and legitimate part of the interaction.

---

## When to use it

- Preparing for a commercial negotiation where revealing your walk-away point or timeline would significantly weaken your position
- Positioning a product, service, or organization in a market and deciding what to make public vs. keep internal
- Sensing that your opponent is managing information against you and wanting to read their signals critically
- About to enter a situation where what you reveal early shapes your entire leverage position for weeks or months
- Want to pressure-test whether your current information posture is consistent — whether you're inadvertently signaling things you're trying to conceal

---

## When *not* to use it

**Do not use this framework for fraud, manipulation of vulnerable parties, or deception that causes disproportionate harm.** Managing information is legitimate in commercial contexts where both parties understand they are in a negotiation and have the means to protect their interests. It is not legitimate when one party has no ability to protect themselves, when the deception involves material facts required for informed consent, or when the mechanism is outright fabrication rather than selective disclosure.

**Don't deploy deception that can't be maintained.** The nearest neighbor: `/strategy-intelligence` — which tells you what your opponent currently believes about you, and therefore what you're actually managing. Without that foundation, you're designing an impression without knowing whether it contradicts information they already have. A collapsed deception is worse than transparency.

---

## Full example

**Input:**
> I'm negotiating a commercial lease for office space. I've been looking for 18 months and this is the best space I've found — I'm genuinely ready to sign. The landlord knows I've been in the market a long time (we've toured multiple spaces they manage). My concern is that if they know how motivated I am and how long I've been searching, they'll have no incentive to move on rent or terms. What should I reveal, and what should I protect?

---

**Output:**

### Deception Analysis

**Information to Protect**

The following information, if known by the landlord, would directly reduce your leverage:

1. *The 18-month search duration* — this signals high motivation and low alternatives. A party who has been searching 18 months and hasn't signed is either very picky (power signal) or unable to close (weakness signal). In this context, given you're ready to sign, it will be read as the latter.

2. *The fact that this is "the best space you've found"* — as soon as you communicate that this is your top choice, you have eliminated your most important piece of leverage: the credible threat to walk away. If they know you think this is the best option, they know walking away costs you more than it costs them.

3. *Your timeline urgency* — if you have a lease expiring, a team growing, or any pressure to move in by a specific date, that information is worth protecting. Timeline pressure is a negotiating liability.

4. *Your budget ceiling* — they will probe for the maximum you're willing to pay. This should never be stated. Your opening position and your walk-away point are both yours to set; they are not obligated information.

**Advantageous False Impressions**

The following beliefs, if held by the landlord, would benefit your negotiating position:

1. *That you have one or two other serious alternatives in play* — a party with alternatives is not desperate. This does not require lying about specific spaces; it can be created through your behavior (asking about terms as if you're comparing, not closing as if you're sold).

2. *That you are a slow-moving, methodical decision-maker* — 18 months of searching can be reframed. "I take my time with major commitments" is a different story from "I've been desperately searching for a year and a half." One signals selectivity; the other signals desperation.

3. *That your timeline is flexible* — even if you have soft urgency, signaling that you're not under a clock reduces the landlord's ability to create artificial deadline pressure to push you to close.

**Credible Signals**

Actions and statements that create the right impressions without fabrication:

- *Ask detailed, comparative questions* about the lease terms in ways that imply you're comparing against alternatives: "In the other spaces we're looking at, the tenant improvement allowance has been structured as X — is that something you do here?" This signals active alternatives without claiming them explicitly.
- *Take time to respond* to their proposals — not days, but 24–48 hours. A party who responds immediately to every proposal signals eagerness. A party who responds after consideration signals that they are weighing options.
- *Reference a process* in your first meeting: "We're working through a short list of spaces and expecting to make a decision in the next few weeks." This creates a timeline on your terms, not theirs.
- *Your broker (if you have one) can carry signals you cannot.* A broker who mentions to the landlord's broker that "my client has a couple of options on the table" is a credible signal in industry context; a tenant saying the same thing directly is less credible. Use the principal-agent structure.

**Consistency Check**

The information barrier you're protecting is the "motivated, no alternatives" picture. The consistency risks:

- *The 18-month search history is already known* to this landlord — they've seen you tour multiple spaces. You cannot make that disappear; you can only reframe it. Don't try to pretend you're a new entrant to the market; instead, lean into the "methodical and selective" reframe, which is consistent with the observable fact.
- *Team members or colleagues present at tours* may signal enthusiasm in ways you wouldn't. Brief anyone attending negotiations on what's appropriate to express. Enthusiasm in a walkthrough is difficult to contain; enthusiasm in a lease term meeting is a negotiating liability.
- *Email chains and document requests* may inadvertently signal urgency — if you ask for a lease draft "as soon as possible" or respond outside business hours, these are behavioral signals.
- *If you claim alternatives exist and they probe specifically* — "which spaces are you looking at?" — you need a prepared response that is truthful and non-revealing. "We're not sharing our shortlist, but we're working through a few options in this area" is honest and protective.

**Counter-Deception Assessment**

The landlord and their broker are likely managing their own information position. Common signals to read critically:

- *"We have another party very interested in this space"* — this is the landlord's version of your alternatives claim. It may be true; it may be standard practice. Ask: when did they first mention it? Was it before or after you signaled interest? Urgency created suspiciously close to your expressed enthusiasm is often manufactured.
- *"We're not in a position to move on rent"* — this means "we haven't seen enough leverage to move on rent yet." It is a negotiating position, not a factual statement about their economics. Their incentive is to fill the space; an empty space costs them real money.
- *Timeline pressure tactics* — "the lease needs to be signed by end of month" or "we have another viewing next week." These are designed to close your deliberation window. Treat them as information to gather, not as deadlines you must meet on their schedule.
- *The friendly rapport build* — in commercial real estate, extended relationship-building conversations before getting to terms are sometimes genuine and sometimes designed to create a sense of obligation that makes it uncomfortable to press hard on terms. Notice which information surfaces during "friendly" conversation and treat it the same way you'd treat anything said at the table.
