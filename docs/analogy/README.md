# 🌉 Analogy

> *Every solved problem is a borrowed solution waiting to be found.*

---

## What this category is

Analogy tools apply rigorous thinking to comparisons — importing solutions from other domains, testing where a metaphor breaks down, identifying the structural correspondence between two situations, and using a different field's lens to see what your own expertise hides from you.

Most analogical thinking is casual: "this is like that." The analogy toolkit makes it systematic: *in what ways* is this like that? *Where* does the comparison break? What *specific* solutions from the source domain transfer, and what doesn't?

---

## When to reach for it

- Stuck on a problem that your domain's standard approaches haven't solved
- Using a metaphor or analogy to make a case — want to verify it actually holds
- Need fresh perspective that doesn't come from inside the problem space
- Making a decision that feels like something you've seen before, but aren't sure the comparison is valid
- Want to find who has solved a structurally similar problem, possibly in a completely different field

---

## When *not* to reach for it

**Don't use analogy when the situation is genuinely novel.** Forced analogies produce confidence without accuracy. If you can't find a domain that's genuinely structurally similar, the analogy work may mislead more than it helps.

**Don't mistake surface similarity for structural similarity.** Two things that look alike on the surface often have completely different underlying dynamics. [structure-mapping](structure-mapping.md) exists specifically to test this — run it before relying on any analogy for a consequential decision.

The nearest neighbor: [Historical](../historical/) — which finds precedents from the past rather than importing mechanisms from other domains.
