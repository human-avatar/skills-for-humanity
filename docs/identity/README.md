# 🪞 Identity

> *Stated values are aspirational. Operative values are revealed by decisions under pressure.*

---

## What this category is

Identity tools apply scrutiny to what you or your organization actually stands for — distinguishing stated values from operative ones, testing whether a direction is genuinely aligned with mission or rationalizing a departure from it, and asking what a person of genuine integrity would do. These are not soft or philosophical tools; they surface the gap between what you claim to be and what your decisions reveal.

The uncomfortable core of this category: most values drift happens through a thousand individually defensible decisions, each of which made sense in isolation.

---

## When to reach for it

- A decision feels uncomfortable but you can't articulate why
- Wondering whether a direction is genuinely aligned with purpose or quietly departing from it
- Want to test a decision against character rather than calculation
- Values seem to be in practice different from the values on the wall
- About to rationalize something that might not deserve rationalization
- Gut says no but the business case says yes

---

## When *not* to reach for it

**Don't confuse identity with preference.** "We've always done it this way" is not a values test; it's inertia. Identity tools test against *committed values*, not habits.

**Don't substitute identity tools for ethical analysis.** "Is this who we want to be?" and "Is this ethical?" are different questions. The former is about internal consistency; the latter involves moral frameworks. Use [Ethics](../ethics/) for stakeholder impact analysis.

The nearest neighbor: [Ethics](../ethics/) — which applies moral frameworks to stakeholder impact. Identity tools test internal consistency with what *you* have committed to. A decision can pass an ethics check and still violate your mission; they're different questions.
