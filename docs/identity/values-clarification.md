# identity-values-clarification

> Surfaces the actual operative values revealed by decisions — distinguishing what is stated from what behavior reveals.

**Trigger:** `/identity-values-clarification`

---

## What it does

Examines recent decisions — especially hard ones where something was sacrificed — to identify the actual hierarchy of values. Compares stated values to operative values. The pattern in decisions reveals what is actually valued, regardless of what's on the wall.

---

## When to use

- "What do we actually stand for?"
- Stated values and actual decisions feel misaligned
- Values alignment check before a significant decision
- "What do our decisions reveal?"

---

## Example

**Input:**
> Our stated values are: Customer First, Speed, Integrity, and Team. Analyze these recent decisions: (1) We shipped a feature we knew had UX issues because the deadline was fixed. (2) We let a high-performer who was disrespectful to colleagues stay for 6 months before acting. (3) We disclosed a service outage to customers within 2 hours and offered refunds proactively. (4) We pushed back on a customer request that would have required a month of engineering for a $20k deal.

**Output:**

**Decision analysis:**

| Decision | Operative value revealed |
|---|---|
| Shipped rough feature to meet deadline | Speed > Customer First (in this context) |
| Tolerated disrespect for 6 months | Retention of talent > Team safety |
| Disclosed outage quickly and proactively | Integrity (strong signal — this had a cost) |
| Pushed back on low-ROI customer request | Speed/Focus > Customer First (but well-reasoned) |

**Actual value hierarchy as revealed:**

1. **Integrity** — the outage disclosure was costly and unprompted. This is a strong signal that integrity is a genuine operative value, not just stated.
2. **Speed/Deadlines** — two of four decisions show speed winning when it conflicted with quality or team wellbeing.
3. **Customer First** — honored when it doesn't cost too much (outage disclosure), overridden when it does (rough UX at deadline).
4. **Team** — the disrespect tolerance is a clear signal that "Team" as a value is underweighted when it requires a difficult personnel conversation.

**Gap between stated and operative:**

"Customer First" is stated first but operates third. "Team" is stated but didn't protect team members from a disrespectful colleague for 6 months.

**This isn't an indictment — it's a map.** Every organization's operative values differ somewhat from stated ones. The value of this analysis is knowing where the gaps are before they create trust problems. Specifically: employees who heard "Team" as a value and then watched the colleague tolerated for 6 months have a trust debt that's real, even if unspoken.

**Recommendation:**
Either update the stated values to match what the organization actually prioritizes (honest, reduces cognitive dissonance), or make a deliberate decision to close the gaps (harder, requires accountability). The current state — stated values that diverge from decisions — creates the most confusion.
