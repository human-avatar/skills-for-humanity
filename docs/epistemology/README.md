# Epistemology

> *Knowing how to know is different from knowing things. Epistemology is the meta-layer.*

---

## What this category is

Epistemology tools examine the nature, structure, and limits of knowledge itself. They don't ask "is this claim true?" — they ask "what kind of truth could this claim even have? What would justify believing it? How certain can you honestly be? And can it even be known?"

The toolkit handles distinct types of epistemic work: classifying what kind of knowing is in play (knowledge-types), examining what would actually justify a belief (justification), auditing confidence levels across a domain (epistemic-status), and finding the boundaries of what can be established at all (limits).

This is philosophy made practical. Epistemology here is not an academic exercise — it's what you reach for when ordinary investigation isn't settling the question, when you want to be genuinely honest about what you know vs. what you're assuming, or when you suspect the question itself is part of the problem.

---

## When to reach for it

- You're uncertain what *kind* of claim you're dealing with — empirical? definitional? value judgment?
- You want to be honest about your confidence level rather than default to sounding certain
- Investigation has been thorough but the question still feels unsettled — you may have hit a structural limit
- A plan or analysis rests on a foundation you haven't examined
- Someone (including yourself) is treating assumption as fact, or testimony as direct evidence
- A question seems answerable but nothing you do makes it clearer — the question may be malformed

---

## When *not* to reach for it

**Don't reach for epistemology when what you need is practical evidence-gathering.** If the question is answerable and you just haven't investigated, investigate. Epistemology is for after — or when the shape of the problem suggests investigation won't be enough.

**Don't use epistemic humility as a substitute for deciding.** Rigorous calibration of uncertainty is not an excuse for indecision — it's a precondition for better decisions. Knowing that something is "assumed" rather than "known" doesn't mean you can't act on it. It means you should act while flagging the assumption and building in a way to test it.

The nearest-neighbor trap: **Epistemology is not skepticism.** The tools here don't conclude "we can't know anything." They're precise about *what* can be known and *how* — which usually means you can establish more than you thought, about fewer things than you claimed. The output is sharper, more honest analysis — not paralysis.
