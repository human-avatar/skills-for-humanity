# Psychology

> *People don't behave rationally. Psychology is the toolkit for understanding why — and working with it.*

---

## What this category is

Psychology tools apply cognitive and behavioral frameworks to diagnose why people think, feel, and act as they do — and how to influence and change behavior. The category covers cognitive biases (where fast thinking goes wrong), motivation (what actually drives behavior beneath the surface explanation), heuristics (when to trust vs. override intuitive judgment), persuasion (what actually changes minds and drives action), and behavior change (what shifts entrenched habits and defaults).

The scope is cognitive and behavioral psychology — the science of how minds work and how behavior is formed, maintained, and changed. This is not clinical psychology: no DSM frameworks, no therapeutic models, no diagnostic labels. It's a practitioner's toolkit for anyone trying to understand, influence, or change human behavior.

---

## When to reach for it

- Behavior doesn't make sense and you need to understand what's actually driving it
- A decision or belief may be distorted by cognitive forces that are invisible from the inside
- You're trying to change someone's mind or get buy-in and your arguments aren't landing
- A habit or behavioral pattern persists despite genuine intention to change it
- You want to know whether to trust your gut or override it with analysis
- You need to understand what someone *actually* wants, versus what they say they want

---

## When *not* to reach for it

**Don't reach for psychology when the real issue is structural or incentive-based.** If a team is underperforming because the incentive structure creates misaligned individual interests, the fix is to change the structure — not to understand each person's motivation profile better. When behavior is a rational response to a poorly designed system, [game-theory](../game-theory/) and [social](../social/) tools are more useful.

**Don't use persuasion tools to manipulate rather than influence.** Persuasion and manipulation share many of the same mechanisms. The distinction is that persuasion works with the audience's judgment — it gives them genuine reasons to update their view. Manipulation bypasses judgment — it exploits biases, creates false urgency, or manufactures social proof. Psychology tools are designed for the former; using them for the latter degrades trust and, when detected, produces the opposite of the intended effect.

The nearest neighbor trap: **Psychology is not social dynamics.** Psychology focuses on the individual mind — what's driving this person's behavior, what biases are active in this person's thinking, how does this person change. The [social](../social/) category handles group behavior, power dynamics, and coalition — how groups form, how norms propagate, who has leverage over whom. They're complementary: when behavior sits at the individual/group boundary (a leader whose behavior is shaping group norms, a team decision that's being distorted by in-group bias), use both.
