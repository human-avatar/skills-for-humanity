# 🔄 Systems

> *Pushing harder in the obvious direction often makes things worse. The leverage is somewhere else.*

---

## What this category is

Systems tools apply Donella Meadows' systems thinking to complex, dynamic situations — mapping the feedback loops that drive behavior, recognizing recurring system archetypes, understanding why systems produce results no one intended, and finding where small interventions create large, lasting change. These tools exist because systems are counterintuitive: the obvious intervention frequently produces the opposite of its intended effect.

The discipline: understand the structure before intervening. Structure determines behavior. Trying to change behavior without changing structure produces temporary results at best.

---

## When to reach for it

- A situation keeps returning to the same state despite repeated interventions
- A policy or fix that seemed sensible produced unexpected side effects
- You need to know where to intervene for maximum effect, not just what to do
- A situation has a familiar dynamic that you can't quite name
- System behavior is producing results no one designed or intended
- You want to understand what's driving current behavior before changing it

---

## When *not* to reach for it

**Don't use systems tools when the situation is simple and linear.** If A causes B and there are no feedback loops, you don't need systems thinking — you need [causality mapping](../logic/causality-mapping.md) in the logic toolkit.

**Systems thinking is not an excuse for analysis paralysis.** The goal is to understand the system well enough to find the high-leverage intervention — not to understand everything before acting.

The nearest neighbor: [Resource](../resource/) — specifically bottleneck analysis, which applies Theory of Constraints to operational throughput. Systems tools work at a more structural, longer-horizon level.
