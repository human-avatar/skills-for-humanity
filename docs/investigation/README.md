# Investigation

> *Knowing something is different from having heard it. Investigation is how you close that gap.*

---

## What this category is

Investigation tools apply practical methodology to the question of whether claims are true. They cover the full span of truth-finding work: tracing a claim back to its origin, decomposing complex assertions into their hidden sub-claims, evaluating whether the evidence is actually good, generating rival explanations that fit the same observations, and checking whether apparently corroborating sources are genuinely independent.

The unifying principle: a claim being widely repeated, confidently asserted, or intuitively plausible is not evidence that it is true. Investigation is the discipline of establishing, rather than assuming. It is distinct from research (which finds information) and from epistemology (which asks philosophical questions about the nature of knowledge) — investigation is hands-on methodology for determining whether what you've heard is actually true.

---

## When to reach for it

- A claim matters for a decision you're about to make and you haven't actually verified it
- You've repeated something and realize you don't know where it came from
- Evidence for a position feels thin, circular, or like it all traces back to the same source
- You need to stress-test a belief before staking real resources on it
- Something is "well established" in your field and you want to know whether that's actually warranted
- You have a hypothesis and want to know if something else could explain the observations equally well
- You want to know whether multiple sources you have are genuinely independent or just amplifying each other

---

## When *not* to reach for it

**Don't use investigation tools when the claim is definitional or unfalsifiable.** "Innovation is about culture, not process" is not an empirical claim — it's a definitional stance. You can't investigate it because there's no evidence that would settle it. Investigation works on claims that could in principle be shown to be false.

**Don't reach for investigation when you need philosophical grounding in what it means to know something.** If the question is "how can I ever be confident in any claim?" or "what is the nature of evidence?" — that's epistemology. Investigation assumes you're trying to establish whether a specific claim is true, not to resolve the foundations of knowledge itself.

The nearest neighbor trap: Investigation is not research. Research finds information; investigation evaluates whether information is actually true. You can spend three hours doing research and emerge with more confidently held misinformation than when you started, if you're not distinguishing between finding claims and establishing them. The question investigation asks at every step is: how do we know this?
