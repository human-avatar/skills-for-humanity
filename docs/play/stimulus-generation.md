# play-stimulus-generation

> Introduces a random, unrelated element to break mental fixation — forcing new associations that bypass the groove of familiar thinking.

**Trigger:** `/play-stimulus-generation`

---

## What it does

Takes a stuck problem, introduces a genuinely unrelated stimulus (word, object, concept), lists its attributes systematically, forces connections between each attribute and the problem without filtering, then develops the most promising connection into a concrete idea. Works because stuck thinking keeps activating the same conceptual neighborhood — the random stimulus forces the mind outside it.

---

## When to use

- "Break the fixation"
- "Random stimulus"
- "I keep coming back to the same ideas"
- "Unstick this"
- "Force new associations"
- Any brainstorm that's going in circles or producing predictable outputs

## When NOT to use

- When the goal is structured creative exploration with multiple frameworks — use `creativity-brainstorm`
- When the goal is inverting a constraint — use `play-constraint-inversion`
- When the goal is imagining the worst outcome and reversing it — use `play-worst-case-reversal`
- When the goal is seeing through a specific other perspective — use `play-perspective-reversal`

---

## Example

**Input:**
> We're designing a loyalty program for a grocery chain. Every idea we come up with is some version of "points that turn into discounts." We've been going in circles for an hour. Introduce a random stimulus.

**Output:**

---

**Stuck Problem:** Loyalty program design for a grocery chain. Stuck groove: variations on points-to-discounts. Haven't escaped the transaction-reward paradigm.

**Random Stimulus:** Coral reef

---

**Stimulus Attributes:**

1. Built by small organisms that each make tiny contributions, accumulating over time into something much larger than any individual
2. Provides habitat — creates value for other species, not just for itself
3. Dies if the environment becomes wrong — sensitive to conditions, not just to effort
4. Visible from the outside only as a mass; experienced from within as individual micro-environments
5. Resilient when diverse, fragile when monoculture
6. Grows slowly in steady conditions; can bleach suddenly under stress
7. Attracts and retains by being genuinely useful, not by creating exit barriers

---

**Forced Connections**

| Attribute | Connection to the Stuck Problem | Worth Developing? |
|---|---|---|
| Built by small contributions accumulating into something larger | Loyalty built through micro-actions (recipe saves, product reviews, shopping list sharing) rather than just purchases — each small act builds toward something bigger | **Yes** — reframes loyalty from transaction-counting to participation |
| Provides habitat for other species | A loyalty program that creates value for the community around the shopper — recommendations shared with friends, group shopping lists, community food boards | **Yes** — loyalty as community infrastructure, not individual rewards |
| Dies if the environment becomes wrong | Loyalty programs fail not from lack of rewards but from wrong conditions (checkout friction, poor product quality, bad store experience) — the program is irrelevant if the environment is hostile | Maybe — diagnostic insight more than design direction |
| Resilient when diverse, fragile when monoculture | A loyalty program that has many ways to earn and participate survives better than one that relies on only purchase frequency | Maybe — directional |
| Attracts by being genuinely useful, not exit barriers | Design the loyalty program so people would use it even if there were no rewards — because the core features (shopping lists, purchase history, recipes) are intrinsically valuable | **Yes** — highest leverage |
| Visible as mass, experienced as micro-environments | The loyalty program feels different to different shoppers — a new parent sees baby product deals, a home cook sees recipe-based recommendations — personalized experience within a shared structure | **Yes** |

---

**Most Promising Direction:**

The **"useful without rewards"** connection. Current loyalty programs create exit barriers: you have points, so you feel locked in. The coral reef doesn't trap fish — fish stay because the habitat is genuinely good. A grocery loyalty program designed so that the core features (purchase history, shopping lists, preference tracking, recipe integration) are intrinsically useful — regardless of points — creates genuine loyalty rather than lock-in. Users who would keep using the app even if points disappeared are genuinely loyal; users who stay only for points are one competitive offer away from leaving.

---

**Developed Idea:**

A loyalty program where membership = access to a personal grocery intelligence layer: searchable purchase history, smart shopping list that learns preferences, meal planning tied to what's on sale, dietary tracking with trend insights, and family-shared lists. Points and discounts exist, but they're secondary to the utility layer. The headline offering at signup: "Your grocery memory — everything you've ever bought, how often, trends in your spending, what went well." This is retention through utility, not through points anxiety. The grocery chain gets the data advantage; the customer gets something they'd actually miss.
