# play-worst-case-reversal

> Deliberately designs the worst possible version, then reverses each failure mode into a design principle.

**Trigger:** `/s4h-play-worst-case-reversal`

---

## What it does

Generates the worst possible version of something — maximum failure, frustration, or harm — without filters. Then reverses each failure mode: if X makes it terrible, NOT-X is a design principle. The worst version is the clearest map to the best version.

---

## When to use

- Safe brainstorming keeps producing safe ideas
- Need to surface what NOT to do before knowing what to do
- A team is being too polite to say what they actually think
- "Reverse brainstorm"

---

## Example

**Input:**
> We're designing a new employee onboarding experience. Let's run a worst-case reversal.

**Output:**

**The worst possible employee onboarding:**

Day 1: New hire arrives. No one is expecting them. Their laptop hasn't been set up. Their manager is in back-to-back meetings. They sit at a spare desk and read the employee handbook alone for four hours.

Week 1: A series of compliance training modules — legal, security, code of conduct — delivered as 45-minute videos with mandatory quizzes. No context for why any of it matters. No human contact beyond their manager's 30-minute weekly 1:1.

Month 1: The new hire is unclear on their role, their team's priorities, and how success is defined. They haven't met their skip-level. They don't know who to ask for what. Their first project is vague. They haven't been told how to be successful; they've been told the rules.

**Reversed into design principles:**

| Failure | Design Principle |
|---|---|
| No one expecting them | First impression is personal: a human greet, a ready workspace, a manager who's genuinely available |
| Compliance training with no context | Every required training has an explicit "here's why this matters for us specifically" |
| No meeting with skip-level | Skip-level meeting is scheduled before day 1 |
| Unclear role and priorities | Written role clarity document reviewed in week 1, not week 6 |
| Don't know who to ask | Assign a buddy who is not the manager — for the "dumb questions" |
| Not told how to succeed | 30/60/90 day expectations written and shared before start date |

**Most important reversal:**
The worst-case is characterized by the absence of human contact and the presence of compliance process. The design principle is: onboarding is first a relationship experience, second an information experience. Compliance modules should be minimized; human touchpoints should be maximized. The organizational information a new hire needs can be delivered over 3 months; the first week is entirely about whether they feel like they made the right choice.
