# 💰 Economics

> *Incentives explain more behaviour than intentions do.*

---

## What this category is

The economics toolkit applies economic reasoning to the decisions, structures, and systems that shape behaviour. This is not primarily about money — it is about how incentives shape what people do, how choices foreclose alternatives, how costs fall on parties who never made the decision, and why rational individuals so reliably produce irrational collective outcomes.

The intellectual lineage runs from Alfred Marshall's marginal revolution (the relevant question is always the *next* unit, not the average or the total) through A.C. Pigou's welfare economics (when costs fall on people who didn't choose them, markets fail predictably) to Ronald Coase's transaction cost analysis, Daniel Kahneman's behavioural findings on how humans actually reason about costs and losses, and Elinor Ostrom's institutional economics — the evidence that communities can and do govern shared resources without either privatisation or state control. Each skill in this category operationalises one of these foundational insights.

The unifying principle: structure determines behaviour. If you want to understand why things happen as they do, or change how they go, understand the incentive structure. Not the stated intentions. Not the formal goals. The structure.

---

## When to reach for it

- You're evaluating a choice and realise you haven't accounted for what you're giving up to make it
- People are behaving differently from how they're supposed to — and you suspect the incentive structure is pushing them there
- A decision or policy will affect parties who aren't in the room making it
- You need to decide how much to do: whether to expand, cut, or continue a commitment
- A group keeps failing to cooperate despite everyone knowing cooperation would be better
- You're designing a policy, rule, or system and want to check whether the incentives point where you want them to

---

## When *not* to reach for it

**Don't apply economic reasoning when the question is primarily relational, ethical, or emotional.** Economics asks "what does the structure reward?" — not "what does this person value?" or "what is the right thing to do?" For questions about trust, fairness, meaning, identity, or moral obligation, the economics lens can be actively distorting: it frames everything as an incentive problem and misses what actually matters to the people involved. When someone is asking for meaning, giving them a marginal analysis is alienating.

**Economics is not a theory of everything.** Incentives are powerful but incomplete as explanations of behaviour. Social norms, emotional commitments, identity, and intrinsic motivation all drive behaviour in ways that don't reduce to incentive structures — and can be undermined by introducing explicit incentives (Titmuss's famous finding: paying blood donors reduced donation rates). When intrinsic motivation is the mechanism, `/s4h-psychology-motivation` and `/s4h-emotional-motivation-mapping` are better tools than incentive mapping.

The nearest neighbor: [Game Theory](../game-theory/) — the formal analysis of strategic interaction. Economics provides the conceptual framework (incentives, externalities, margin, coordination); game theory provides the mathematical precision (payoff matrices, equilibria, mechanism design). Use economics to understand the structure of the problem; use game theory when you need to reason formally about what specific players will do.
