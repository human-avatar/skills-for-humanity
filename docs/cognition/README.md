# 🧠 Cognition

> *Thinking about thinking is the highest-leverage cognitive skill.*

---

## What this category is

Cognition tools apply cognitive science to the process of thinking itself — not to what you're thinking about, but to how. They address the biological and psychological architecture of the mind: the limits of working memory, the invisible models that shape perception, the capacity to monitor your own understanding, and the economics of attention as a scarce resource.

The intellectual lineage is precise. George Miller established working memory limits in 1956. John Flavell founded metacognition as a formal discipline in the 1970s. John Sweller built Cognitive Load Theory in the 1980s, translating Miller's findings into practical design principles. Philip Johnson-Laird's model theory showed that human reasoning operates through internal mental simulations rather than formal logic. Daniel Kahneman's two-systems framework drew the distinction between fast automatic processing and slow deliberate reasoning. These are not loose metaphors — they are empirically grounded models of how the cognitive system works, and they have direct, actionable implications.

The unifying principle: most cognitive failures are not failures of intelligence or effort. They are failures of design — the wrong information architecture, the wrong attention environment, an invisible model being applied where it doesn't fit, or monitoring that stopped too soon. Fixing the design is almost always higher leverage than trying harder.

## When to reach for it

- You keep losing track of where you are in a complex problem
- Your confidence about something turns out to be miscalibrated — repeatedly
- An approach that should be working keeps not working, and you can't see why
- You're designing a learning experience, communication, or system for other people to use
- A decision or belief feels right but you can't explain the mechanism
- Focus is fragmenting and you don't know where it's going or how to reclaim it
- You suspect your mental model of a situation is outdated or incomplete

---

## When *not* to reach for it

**Don't use cognition tools when the problem is primarily emotional or motivational, not cognitive.** If you understand what to do perfectly well but can't bring yourself to do it, that is not a working memory problem or a metacognitive failure — it is a motivation or resistance problem. Reach for `/s4h-emotional-resistance-diagnosis` or `/s4h-psychology-behavior-change` instead.

**Cognition is not epistemology.** Cognition tools examine the mechanisms of thinking — how attention works, how models shape perception, how working memory operates. Epistemology examines the warrant for specific claims — whether a belief is adequately justified. If your question is "am I thinking well?" reach for cognition. If your question is "is this belief correct and well-supported?", reach for `/s4h-epistemology`.

The nearest neighbor: [Psychology](../psychology/) — which applies behavioural and cognitive frameworks to understand why people act as they do. Psychology is the right category when the question is about someone else's behaviour or about influence; cognition is the right category when the question is about the quality of your own thinking process.

## Related categories

- [Psychology](../psychology/) — for behavioural and motivational questions; when the question is about other people's cognition, or about changing behaviour rather than improving thinking quality
- [Epistemology](../epistemology/) — for questions about the warrant and justification of specific beliefs; the rigour of what you believe, not how you're thinking
- [Mindset](../mindset/) — for the dispositional and attitudinal conditions that make good cognition possible: growth orientation, reframing, flow
- [Decision](../decision/) — for structured decision-making processes; cognition tools address the thinking quality that feeds decisions, decision tools address the decision architecture itself
