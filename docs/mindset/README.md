# Mindset

> *The frame shapes what you see. Mindset skills change the frame.*

---

## What this category is

Mindset tools are deep, philosophy-grade frameworks for orienting toward life, work, challenge, and failure. Each skill encodes a complete methodology — not a motivational summary, but an executable technique grounded in Stoicism, positive psychology, growth mindset research, cognitive behavioral frameworks, and flow theory. The goal in every case is the same: not to think better about a specific object, but to shift the orientation from which you engage with a whole class of situations.

These are not surface-level reframes or motivational prompts. Mindset-stoic encodes the full Stoic practice toolkit with primary-source grounding and a structured process for applying the dichotomy of control, negative visualization, amor fati, and memento mori. Mindset-positive runs a diagnostic audit of all five PERMA dimensions and routes to targeted interventions. Each skill is designed so that a reader can execute the full technique from the instructions alone.

---

## When to reach for it

- Your thinking orientation is making a hard situation harder — not the situation itself but your relationship to it
- You want to choose how to relate to something rather than just react
- You're stuck in a pattern you can clearly see but cannot shift
- You want to design conditions for optimal experience and sustained engagement
- Failure is landing differently than it should — either too personally or not as a learning signal
- Something feels missing in your life but the surface metrics look fine

---

## When *not* to reach for it

**Don't use mindset skills when the problem is external and real.** Reframing a genuinely unjust situation is not the same as fixing it. The Stoic dichotomy of control is not an excuse to accept what should be changed. Cognitive reframing applies to distorted interpretations — not to accurate perceptions of real problems. If the situation is genuinely bad and action is available, start there.

**Don't use reframing to avoid legitimate negative emotions.** Grief, anger, fear, and frustration carry information. They mark what matters, signal threats, and motivate necessary responses. The goal of mindset work is not emotional suppression — it is accurate orientation. Distress that is proportionate to its cause is not a cognitive error. Only distress that is disproportionate — amplified by distortion — warrants restructuring.

The nearest-neighbor trap: **mindset is not motivation.** Motivation skills diagnose what drives and sustains behavior; mindset skills change the *orientation* from which you engage. A person can understand exactly what motivates them (identity, social belonging, intrinsic interest) and still be stuck in a fixed-mindset pattern about failure, or a catastrophizing loop about outcomes. Use both when you need to understand the drivers of behavior *and* shift the frame from which you're engaging with a challenge.
