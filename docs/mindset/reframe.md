# mindset-reframe

> Map the cognitive distortion and generate a more accurate interpretation.

**Trigger:** `/mindset-reframe`

---

## What it does

Applies cognitive reframing as a rigorous methodology — CBT-adjacent but not therapy. Works through the cognitive triangle (thoughts → feelings → behaviors) to identify the specific automatic thought generating disproportionate distress, examine the evidence for and against it, name the cognitive distortion operating (from a reference taxonomy of ten), and generate an alternative interpretation that is equally or better supported by the actual evidence. The goal is not positive thinking — it is accurate thinking. Measures before-and-after distress to test whether the reframe has landed.

---

## When to use

- Stuck in a negative thought loop you can see but can't break
- Automatically interpreting ambiguous situations in the worst possible way
- Distress that feels disproportionate to the situation's actual severity
- Catastrophizing about outcomes — treating unlikely bad scenarios as certain
- Assuming you know what others are thinking and it's negative
- Applying "always/never" language to specific, isolated events

## When *not* to use

**Don't use reframing when the thought is accurate.** If someone is being treated unjustly, reframing the injustice is not the solution — addressing it is. The methodology is designed to correct distortion, not to soften accurate perceptions of genuine problems. Step 3 (evidence examination) must include the evidence that supports the original thought; if the evidence strongly supports it, the right response is action, not reframing.

**Don't use reframing when the real work is acceptance rather than correction.** If someone is grieving a real loss, angry about a genuine wrong, or afraid of something actually dangerous, those emotions carry information. They should be felt and processed, not restructured away. Reframing applies to *amplification* — where a cognitive distortion is making a difficult situation worse than it needs to be. It does not apply to the difficulty itself.

The nearest-neighbor trap: reframing looks like growth mindset but is broader. Growth mindset is a specialized reframe targeting specifically the belief that difficulty reveals fixed incapacity. Reframing covers the full range of cognitive distortions — catastrophizing, mind-reading, all-or-nothing, emotional reasoning, and more. If the thought is specifically "this struggle means I lack the ability for this," use growth mindset. If it's "this situation means disaster" or "my manager definitely hates me," use reframe.

## Example

**Input:**
> I sent a proposal to a senior client last week and haven't heard back. I've been telling myself they hated it, I pitched it wrong, they're probably considering switching providers, and my boss is going to find out I blew this relationship. I know I'm probably overreacting but I can't stop thinking about it.

**Output:**

### The Automatic Thought

**Thought:** *"The silence means the client hated the proposal, I damaged the relationship, and this is going to have serious professional consequences for me."*

**Emotional response:** Anxiety at 8/10

### Evidence Examination

**Evidence FOR the thought:**
- One week without response, which is longer than the person hoped
- No acknowledgment of receipt
- The person had uncertainty about how the proposal would land

**Evidence AGAINST the thought:**
- One week is a normal response time for a senior client reviewing a proposal
- No negative signal has been received — silence is absence of signal, not negative signal
- Clients don't typically switch providers over a single proposal without a direct communication
- The person has not received any feedback suggesting the relationship is at risk
- The client may be traveling, in meetings, or dealing with higher priorities
- The person reports they "probably know" they're overreacting — this suggests the 8/10 intensity is already flagged as disproportionate

### Cognitive Distortion(s)

- **Fortune telling** — Predicting specific negative outcomes (client hated it, switching providers, boss finds out) as established facts rather than possibilities among many
- **Mind reading** — Assuming the client's internal response ("they hated it") without evidence
- **Catastrophizing / magnification** — The chain of consequences being constructed (damaged relationship → boss involvement → professional consequences) amplifies a single ambiguous data point (no response after one week) into a cascading disaster
- **Emotional reasoning** — "I feel anxious, therefore something is wrong." The anxiety is being used as evidence that the silence is negative, when anxiety is a response to uncertainty, not a detector of actual bad news

### Alternative Interpretation

*"One week without a response is normal for a senior client reviewing a proposal. I have received no negative signal about this relationship. The most probable explanations for silence are scheduling and workload, not rejection. I don't yet have information about how the proposal was received."*

**Why this holds:** The alternative requires only what the evidence actually supports: silence plus normal timelines plus absence of negative signal. It does not require pretending the situation is certain to go well — it requires only refusing to treat uncertainty as evidence of the worst case.

### Re-rated Distress

**Original:** 8/10 → **After reframe:** likely 4–5/10

**Assessment:** A rating that halves is meaningful progress. Some anxiety about an unresolved important situation is appropriate — it reflects that this matters. An 8/10 about silence that is consistent with normal timelines is the distortion. A 4–5/10 acknowledges genuine uncertainty without treating that uncertainty as catastrophe. If the rating remains at 7–8 after the reframe has been examined and found to be well-supported, that may indicate the anxiety is coming from somewhere other than this specific thought (a deeper fear about professional standing, for example) that warrants its own examination.

### What to Do Next

The reframe reveals a concrete action: follow up with the client. A brief, professional check-in — "I wanted to make sure you received the proposal and to offer any clarification" — ends the uncertainty that is feeding the anxiety loop. Acting on the accurate interpretation (silence = ambiguous, not negative) produces information. Acting on the distorted interpretation (silence = catastrophe) produces paralysis or overreaction.

Follow-up sent. Response received (or further silence, now with a different status). Either way, the person is dealing with what is actually true rather than with what they have constructed.
