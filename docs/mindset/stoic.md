# mindset-stoic

> The full Stoic toolkit: find what is yours, apply the practice, find equanimity.

**Trigger:** `/s4h-mindset-stoic`

---

## What it does

Applies the Stoic philosophical tradition as a practical, executable methodology — not a summary of Stoic ideas but a process you can run on a real situation. The entry point is the dichotomy of control (what is "up to us" vs. what is not), which determines which of four practices to apply: negative visualization for fear of future loss, amor fati for resistance to what has already happened, memento mori for clarifying what matters, and the view from above for restoring proportion. Grounded in Marcus Aurelius, Epictetus, and Seneca.

---

## When to use

- Caught in worry about outcomes that depend on others' decisions or external events
- Struggling with something that has already happened and cannot be changed
- Distress is disproportionate because the situation *feels* catastrophic but may not actually be
- Want to find equanimity in adversity — not numbness, but genuine stability
- Facing loss, failure, criticism, or illness and want a framework for relating to it

## When *not* to use

**Don't use Stoic framing when the problem is a cognitive distortion about evidence.** If the issue is catastrophizing (interpreting ambiguous events as disasters), mind-reading (assuming others' negative thoughts), or all-or-nothing thinking, the right tool is mindset-reframe — which corrects specific thought distortions through evidence examination. The Stoic toolkit applies the dichotomy of control to *genuinely difficult* situations; it is not the right tool for restructuring *inaccurate* interpretations of situations that are actually neutral or ambiguous.

**Don't use Stoic framing to avoid necessary action.** The dichotomy of control establishes what you cannot govern — but what *is* in your control (your actions, judgments, and choices) remains fully your responsibility. Amor fati is not fatalism. The Stoics were not passive. Marcus Aurelius wrote the *Meditations* while running an empire and fighting on its frontiers. The acceptance of what is outside your control and the vigorous exercise of what is inside it are both required.

The nearest-neighbor trap: Stoicism looks similar to emotional suppression but is structurally different. Emotional suppression denies the feeling. Stoic practice says: yes, this is difficult — and the difficulty does not govern my response. The Stoics called this *eupatheia* (good emotion): not the absence of feeling but feeling grounded in accurate assessment.

## Example

**Input:**
> I've been passed over for a promotion I worked toward for two years. My manager told me the decision was based on "strategic alignment" — which I take to mean politics. I'm devastated and furious. I keep playing it over in my head, alternating between thinking I should have done things differently and thinking the whole system is rigged. I can't seem to let go of it.

**Output:**

### Situation
Passed over for a two-year goal. Explanation given ("strategic alignment") feels inadequate and possibly political. Cycling between self-blame and external blame. Cannot disengage.

### Dichotomy of Control

**In my control:**
- The quality and effort of my work over the past two years
- How I respond to this outcome now
- Whether I seek clarity on what "strategic alignment" actually means
- Whether I choose to stay, leave, or renegotiate the terms of how I work here
- My interpretation of what this means about my capability and my future
- My conduct and character going forward

**Not in my control:**
- The promotion decision (made)
- The criteria actually used (possibly opaque)
- Whether politics were involved (possibly true, possibly not)
- My manager's perception of my strategic alignment
- How this outcome will affect my reputation in the organization

**What this reveals:** Almost all of the suffering in this situation is coming from the items in the right column. The rumination — "should I have done things differently?" and "the system is rigged" — is a continuous attempt to exert control over something that is no longer governable. The decision is made. The things that remain fully in this person's hands are substantial: what they do next, what they seek to understand, and how they choose to interpret what happened.

### Practice Applied: Amor Fati

The specific pain here is resistance to something that has already happened. Negative visualization would apply if the fear were about a future loss; the view from above would apply if the situation felt cosmically significant. This is amor fati territory: the work is not to accept the outcome as a ceiling — it is to stop fighting a reality that cannot be undone, and from that grounded position, act.

*Seneca:* "Recede in te ipse quantum potes" — "Retreat into yourself as much as you can." Not as withdrawal, but as the recognition that the interior — judgment, choice, character — is where your actual life happens.

The amor fati practice applied here: What if this is not something that happened *to* you but something that happened *for* your life — not because the promotion outcome was good, but because how you respond to it is now yours to shape completely? The two years of work produced skills, relationships, and capability. No decision committee can revoke those. The outcome was disappointing. The capability is real.

The specific reframe: not "I was robbed of something I deserved" (which requires the system to acknowledge the injustice — not in your control) but "I invested two years building something real, the outcome was not what I wanted, and what I choose to do with that is entirely mine." Amor fati does not require approving of what happened. It requires refusing to make your peace contingent on the past being different.

### The Reframe
The promotion decision cannot be undone and its internal rationale cannot be fully known. Investing energy in the question "should I have done things differently?" is spending the present trying to rewrite the past — which is not in your control. The question that *is* in your control: given what you now know about how decisions are made here, what do you choose to do? That question has genuine purchase. The other does not.

### The Stoic Verdict
*Epictetus, Enchiridion 5:* "Men are disturbed not by the things which happen, but by the opinions about the things: for example, death is nothing terrible, for if it were, it would have seemed so to Socrates; for the opinion about death, that it is terrible, is the terrible thing."

The promotion outcome is an external event. The devastation comes from the interpretation: that the outcome is evidence of worth, that the failure of the system to recognize contribution is a verdict on the contribution itself. The Stoic observation: the work was real. The capability developed is real. A decision committee's assessment of "strategic alignment" is not a measurement of either. What you have built remains. What was taken was only a title.
