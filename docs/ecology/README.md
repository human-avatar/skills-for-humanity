# 🌿 Ecology

> *Everything is connected; the question is how tightly.*

---

## What this category is

Ecology tools apply the concepts of ecosystem science to any complex system — organisations, markets, technologies, communities, and supply chains. The discipline originated with Arthur Tansley's 1935 formulation of the ecosystem concept and was deepened by Howard Odum's work on energy flows, Robert Paine's keystone species experiments, Joseph Connell's studies of community interaction, and C.S. Holling's adaptive cycle model. What these thinkers share is a commitment to understanding systems through their relationships, their limits, their structural dependencies, and their developmental trajectories — not through the properties of individual components in isolation.

The unifying principle: structure determines behaviour. You cannot understand why a system behaves the way it does by studying its parts independently. The relationships between parts — who depends on whom, how tightly, with what capacity, at what stage of development — are the explanation. Ecology tools make those relationships explicit so you can reason about them rather than be surprised by them.

The toolkit covers four problems. First: limits — every system has a carrying capacity, and approaching it without awareness is how overshoot happens. Second: structural indispensability — some entities hold systems together disproportionately; their removal does not simply reduce system capacity, it restructures it. Third: dependency webs — changes propagate through systems along dependency pathways that are often invisible until something breaks. Fourth: developmental stage — systems move through predictable phases of growth, consolidation, release, and reorganisation; the right strategy depends entirely on which phase you are in.

---

## When to reach for it

- A system is growing and you want to understand whether and when it will hit a structural ceiling
- A key person, vendor, platform, or capability might be lost and you need to understand the structural consequences
- A change is being planned — a reorganisation, a product decision, a market entry — and you need to trace its propagation through a dependency web
- An organisation or industry feels like it is at an inflection point and you need a framework to read what kind of transition is underway
- A situation keeps producing the same problems despite repeated interventions, suggesting unrecognised structural dependencies
- You are trying to identify where to invest in resilience — what the system's current structural vulnerabilities are

---

## When *not* to reach for it

**Don't use ecology tools when the situation is simple, bounded, and linear.** If A causes B with no feedback loops, no dependency web, and no developmental trajectory, ecological thinking adds complexity without insight. Use [causality mapping](../logic/causality-mapping.md) in the logic toolkit instead.

**Ecology is not the same as systems thinking, though they overlap.** Systems tools focus on feedback loop dynamics — what is reinforcing or balancing, what the dominant loop is, where the leverage is in a loop structure. Ecology tools focus on structural relationships — carrying capacity, dependency architecture, keystone indispensability, and developmental stage. If the core question is "why does this keep happening?", systems is likely the right entry point. If the core question is "what holds this together and what changes if we modify it?", ecology is the right entry point.

The nearest neighbor: [Systems](../systems/) — specifically feedback mapping and leverage analysis, which complement ecology tools by explaining the dynamic behavior of the structures that ecology analysis makes visible.
