# ecology-succession

> Reads where a system is in its developmental cycle — and what that means for strategy.

**Trigger:** `/ecology-succession`

---

## What it does

Applies C.S. Holling's adaptive cycle model to any complex system — organisations, markets, industries, technologies, or communities — to identify which of four developmental phases the system is in (rapid growth, conservation, release, or reorganisation) and what that diagnosis implies for strategy and navigation. Draws on the concept of ecological succession, which describes how communities change from pioneer species through consolidation to climax state and through disturbance and back again. The output is a phase diagnosis, a map of the next transition, and a phase-appropriate strategic posture.

---

## When to use

- "What stage are we in, and what does that mean for how we should act?"
- A system feels like it is at an inflection point — the old model is working less well, or everything is changing at once
- Planning strategy and want to understand whether the environment calls for growth, efficiency, resilience, or experimentation
- Trying to understand why a competitor or market is behaving in ways that seem irrational or hard to read
- Post-disruption: making sense of what a collapse or crisis means structurally

---

## Example

**Input:**
> I work at a mid-market HR software company. We were founded in 2012, had strong growth until 2019, then flatlined. The pandemic gave us a bump from remote work adoption but now that's faded. We have 220 staff, a mature product, and good retention. But we've lost the ability to ship new things — everything takes too long, requires too many sign-offs, and we've accumulated so much technical debt that engineering is mostly maintenance. Now AI-native HR tools are launching and we're trying to figure out what to do. What stage are we in and what does that imply?

**Output:**

**System:** Mid-market HR SaaS company, founded 2012, 220 staff, mature product, AI disruption underway.

**Phase Diagnosis**

| Phase Signal | Present? | Evidence |
|--------------|----------|----------|
| Abundant resources / fast growth | No | Growth has flatlined since 2019; pandemic bump has faded |
| Efficiency premium / specialisation | Yes | Strong retention; mature product; process-optimised delivery |
| Accumulated rigidity / high interconnection | Yes | Slow shipping; many sign-offs required; high technical debt; "everything takes too long" |
| Breakdown of accumulated structure | Beginning | AI-native competitors disrupting; the accumulated product and process structure is becoming a liability |
| Experimentation / new forms competing | External, not internal | AI-native entrants are in r-phase; they are the pioneer species in this cycle |

**Current Phase: Late K (conservation) → approaching Ω (release)**

The company is a textbook late-K system. It has accumulated exactly what K-phase systems accumulate: efficiency, specialisation, deep interconnection, and rigidity. The delivery slowness, the sign-off requirements, the technical debt — these are not management failures. They are the structural consequences of optimising for stability and efficiency over a decade. The system is highly adapted to the conditions of 2015-2022. Those conditions are changing.

The AI-native entrants are in r-phase: fast, experimental, cheap to operate, tolerant of imperfection. They are the pioneer species of a new cycle beginning in the ecological space this company currently occupies. The company is not yet in Ω (release phase), but the signals of approaching release are present: external disruption, internal rigidity preventing adaptation, growing sense that the accumulated structure is becoming a constraint rather than an asset.

**Sub-Systems Out of Phase:**

- **Engineering** is arguably already in Ω — technical debt is so accumulated that the old structure is effectively in breakdown, unable to support the workload placed on it.
- **Sales and retention functions** are still in K — mature, efficient, high-performing in the current model. This sub-system will be disrupted later than engineering.

**Next Transition**

| Scenario | Trigger Conditions | Timeline | First Signals | Strategic Implication |
|----------|-------------------|----------|--------------|----------------------|
| Managed K→r transition | Deliberate structural release before external forcing | 12-24 months if started now | Slowing engineering throughput deliberately to address technical debt; explicit permission to experiment | Navigate the transition on your own terms; maintain customer base while rebuilding for the next cycle |
| Unmanaged Ω (forced release) | AI-native competitor captures 20%+ of new business; customer churn accelerates; talent leaves | 18-36 months at current trajectory | Accelerating churn in the 0-24 month customer cohort; engineer attrition; sales cycle lengthening | Crisis-driven reorganisation; survival mode; harder to preserve what's worth keeping |

**Phase-Appropriate Strategic Posture:**

A late-K system approaching Ω has one primary strategic imperative: *deliberately release accumulated rigidity before being forced to by external disruption.* This is counterintuitive. K-phase instincts are to preserve and defend (protect the existing structure, optimise for efficiency, reduce risk). Those instincts will accelerate the unmanaged scenario.

What deliberate release looks like in this context:

1. **Technical debt as priority one.** Engineering must spend a defined period — 6-12 months — primarily reducing accumulated constraints, not adding features. This will feel like falling behind. It is the prerequisite for the next cycle.

2. **Explicit permission for r-phase experiments alongside the core product.** A small, structurally separated team operating with pioneer-phase rules (fast, undocumented, allowed to fail) to explore what the company's r-phase looks like. Keep it small and separate — attempting to run r-phase experiments within the K-phase governance structure will kill them.

3. **Identify what from the K phase is worth carrying into the next cycle.** Not everything accumulated in K phase is rigidity — some of it is genuine value: customer relationships, domain knowledge, distribution, trust. Inventory what should be preserved through the transition versus deliberately released.

4. **Prepare the customer base.** Customers in a stable, mature product will experience the transition as disruption. Manage this proactively.

**Panarchy Context:**

The HR software market as a whole is in Ω — the AI disruption is a market-level release event, not just a company-level one. This matters because the larger system's release is creating the conditions for the company's own Ω to be pulled forward. You cannot simply wait this out — the market-level transition will force the issue. The strategic question is whether to manage your own release ahead of the market forcing it, or to let the market forcing determine the timeline.
