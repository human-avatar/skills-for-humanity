# `/s4h-writing-arc-design`

> *The ending is not the change. The ending is the consequence of the change.*

**Category:** [Writing](README.md) · **Command:** `/s4h-writing-arc-design`

---

## What it does

Maps character arcs and thematic arcs by aligning start state, pressure points, and end state — diagnosing where the external plot and internal change are running on independent tracks rather than one causing the other. Produces an arc map with specific diagnosis and, where the arc is unearned, targeted interventions.

---

## When to use it

- A character's change in the final act feels sudden or unearned
- An ending resolves the external plot but doesn't feel emotionally satisfying
- The character seems to change because the story requires it, not because the events forced it
- The plot and the character arc feel disconnected from each other
- The story has a clear theme but the ending doesn't feel like an argument about it
- A "redemption arc" or similar transformation arc isn't landing

---

## When *not* to use it

**Don't use this for static characters.** Not all characters arc. Some characters are defined by their resistance to change — and that resistance is itself the arc. A character who refuses to change in a story about transformation can be profoundly effective. Arc design is for stories where a transformation is intended and is not landing.

**Don't use this without a character profile.** The arc endpoint must flow from the wound and defence established in character development. Designing an arc for a character whose wound and defence haven't been mapped is designing a destination without knowing the starting point. Use `/s4h-writing-character-development` first.

The nearest neighbor: [`/s4h-writing-plot-structure`](plot-structure.md) — which addresses the external events. Arc design and plot structure must be diagnosed together: the external structure is the mechanism that generates the pressure points that force the internal change. A well-designed arc with a broken plot structure will not be delivered.

---

## Full example

**Input:**
> My novel is a redemption arc for Marcus, a defence attorney who has spent his career getting guilty people off on technicalities. He knows they're guilty. He tells himself he's defending the system, not the people. In the final chapter, after his last client — a man he knows killed his wife — goes free, Marcus suddenly decides to leave the law and dedicate himself to victims' advocacy. Readers say the change feels sudden and unearned.

---

**Output:**

### Arc Map: Marcus

**Start State:**
Marcus believes: *the system is the thing worth defending, not the people inside it.* This is his operating philosophy, and it has been carefully constructed to be impenetrable — because it is correct, in a procedural sense. Defence attorneys do defend the system. The system requires that the guilty get adequate representation. Marcus has made himself the highest-quality version of a necessary institution.

The wound underneath this: Marcus cannot remember the last time he made a decision based on what he believed was right rather than what the system required. He has so thoroughly identified with the role that the role has replaced the person. What he cannot afford to examine: that the system he is defending has not, in his hands, served justice in any case he can name.

**End State:**
The intended end state — victims' advocacy — is not wrong, but it is arrived at as a conclusion without having been forced by anything. It is a decision Marcus makes, not a transformation he undergoes.

The correct end state for this arc: Marcus arrives at the belief that *the system is only worth defending when the person who defends it is willing to be wrong.* Or more precisely: *I have not been defending anything. I have been hiding.* The end state is not a career change — it is the collapse of the philosophical structure he has built to avoid knowing this.

**Pressure Points — Current State:**

Reviewing what the novel currently does:
1. Marcus wins a case for a client he knows is guilty. (Chapter 3) — This is *not* a pressure point because Marcus has done this many times. It is background, not escalation.
2. Marcus's daughter tells him she is ashamed of him. (Chapter 11) — This lands but Marcus deflects it: "I'm doing what I have to do." The defence holds.
3. The victim's sister confronts Marcus outside the courthouse. (Chapter 18) — Marcus is affected but compartmentalises: "I do my job. The system determines the outcome." The defence holds.
4. Marcus's last client goes free and Marcus feels something he can't name. (Chapter 24) — He then decides to leave the law.

*Diagnosis: Three out of four events end with the defence intact. The arc is flat because no event presses hard enough to crack the defence. The change in Chapter 24 is not caused by events — it is an authorial intervention. Marcus decides to change because the story is ending.*

**Pressure Points — What Is Missing:**

For the arc to be earned, three things must happen that currently do not:

1. **The defence must be made untenable by a specific event that Marcus cannot rationalise.** Currently, every event can be absorbed by "I defend the system." What would make this impossible? A specific case where Marcus's procedural victory directly, visibly, immediately results in someone's death or serious harm — something he watches happen as a consequence of his work, in a way the system cannot absorb as collateral damage. Not someone being sad. Someone being hurt, now, because of what he did in the courtroom yesterday.

2. **The wound must be pressed, not the philosophy.** Every event currently attacks his philosophy, which he has armoured. What he hasn't armoured: the question of who he was before he became this. The wound is that somewhere in his history, he made a choice to stop trusting his own moral judgment. *When was that choice? What happened?* Whatever that event is, the story needs a scene that puts Marcus in that moment again — a case that is structurally identical to the one that originally turned him — and forces him to choose again.

3. **The dark night must be an active failure, not a passive discomfort.** Currently the low point is Marcus watching his client go free and feeling "something he can't name." This is passive. For the dark night to be earned, Marcus must try — must do something to stop the outcome or make it right — and fail. The defeat of active effort is the dark night. Discomfort is not.

**External-Internal Alignment:**

| External beat | Internal requirement | Currently delivered? |
|---|---|---|
| Client goes free (Ch 3) | Should crack the philosophy's certainty | No — normalised as background |
| Daughter's shame (Ch 11) | Should press the wound: who did he used to be? | No — deflected by philosophy |
| Victim's sister (Ch 18) | Should make the human cost undeniable | No — deflected by role |
| Last client free (Ch 24) | Should be a consequence of failed active effort | No — passive discomfort |

The external and internal tracks are parallel but not connected. Marcus's philosophy absorbs every external event without transformation.

**Thematic Argument:**

The story seems to argue: *proceduralism without moral engagement is a form of cowardice.* The ending — Marcus entering victims' advocacy — resolves this by having him choose engagement. But the ending does not test the argument at full strength.

The argument at full strength requires: a moment where Marcus has the system on one side and a human being on the other, and the system is winning, and he has to decide whether to let it. The climax should be that decision, not its aftermath.

**Arc Verdict:** Unearned. The change is correct in direction but not in cause. The specific repairs required: (1) add a pressure point that makes the philosophy untenable through specific, immediate consequence; (2) surface the wound through a scene that returns Marcus to the original choice; (3) rebuild the dark night as active failure.
