# `/writing-prose-elevation`

> *Flat prose fails in three specific ways. Fix those three things and the prose rises.*

**Category:** [Writing](README.md) · **Command:** `/writing-prose-elevation`

---

## What it does

Identifies existing strengths in prose and then targets the three primary quality failures — abstraction without grounding, weak verbs, and sensory absence — with specific substitutions and a rewritten sample passage showing the cumulative elevation effect. Preserves the writer's voice while realising its latent quality.

---

## When to use it

- Writing is technically clean but reads as flat or forgettable
- A critique says "competent but not compelling" or "it doesn't come alive"
- An opening chapter or key section isn't drawing readers in despite correct mechanics
- The writing describes experience without recreating it
- Prose passes a line edit without problems but something is still missing
- You want to improve prose quality without imposing a different style

---

## When *not* to use it

**Don't use this before line editing.** A passage that is verbose, passive-heavy, and redundant will not benefit from elevation until the mechanical problems are removed. Clean the mechanics first (see [`/writing-line-editing`](line-editing.md)); then elevate the quality of what remains.

**Don't use this to impose a style.** The goal is to realise the latent quality already present in the writer's voice, not to replace it with a more generic version of "good prose." A prose elevation that makes every passage sound the same, or that overrides the writer's idiosyncrasies, has failed. The rewritten sample should sound like the best version of the writer, not like someone else.

The nearest neighbor: [`/writing-line-editing`](line-editing.md) — which addresses mechanical failures at the sentence level. These two skills work in sequence: line editing first (remove problems), prose elevation second (raise quality). Both can be needed without either substituting for the other.

---

## Full example

**Input:**
> Here is the opening chapter of my literary novel. It's been through several drafts. My writing group says it's error-free and well-structured but reads as flat. It feels professional but not alive.
>
> *"Nadia came back to the town where she had grown up after being away for fifteen years. It was smaller than she remembered, or maybe she had just grown bigger. The buildings were old and familiar. The main street had some new shops but most of it was the same. She felt strange to be back. She had complicated feelings about her childhood here. She walked down the main street and looked at things. She noticed the bakery was still there. She got a coffee and sat outside and thought about her life."*

---

**Output:**

### Prose Elevation Report

**Existing Strengths:**

- The emotional core is correct: "smaller than she remembered, or maybe she had just grown bigger" is a strong observation — it does two things at once (physical fact + psychological insight). This is the passage's best moment. *Do not override this line.*
- The structure is appropriately quiet for a return-home opening. The restraint is right; the prose isn't trying to overstate its drama.
- The instinct to locate her in a specific place (the bakery, the coffee) is correct — the specificity instinct is there but not yet fully deployed.

---

**Abstraction Instances:**

1. "She felt strange to be back" — This is pure summary. What does "strange" look like physically? What does Nadia actually experience?
   > Replacement: Remove the abstract statement and show the specific sensation. What does the strangeness feel like in the body? Example approach: *"She kept having to recalibrate — kept expecting a turn that now went somewhere different, a door where there was now a window."*

2. "She had complicated feelings about her childhood here" — "Complicated feelings" is not a feeling. It is a label for a category of feelings.
   > Replacement: Remove entirely. The complication should emerge through what she sees and how she responds to it. Telling the reader her feelings are complicated prevents them from feeling the complication themselves.

3. "She looked at things" — This is abstract movement through space. What specifically does she look at? More importantly: what does she *not* look at? What is she avoiding?
   > Replacement: Name the specific thing she looks at — and the specific thing she walks past without looking at.

---

**Verb Replacements:**

1. "came back to the town" → stronger options: "returned to" (more precise, less casual); or a more specific verb that carries the quality of the return — "drove into," "arrived in," implying mode of arrival and approach.

2. "It was smaller than she remembered" — "was" is the weakest possible verb here. The observation that follows it is strong; give it a stronger verb.
   > Consider: "It looked smaller than she remembered" (adds her perspective explicitly) or simply restructure: "Everything was the size it had always been, she supposed. She had just gotten larger."

3. "She walked down the main street and looked at things" — "walked" and "looked" are both underloaded.
   > "She moved down the main street" or something that carries her pace and manner; "looked" → "checked each frontage as she passed" or "kept her eyes on the far end, not looking at the gaps."

4. "She got a coffee" — "got" is a null verb.
   > "She ordered a coffee" or "She bought a coffee at the counter" — the more specific verb gives the action a texture.

5. "sat outside and thought about her life" — "thought about her life" is the single flattest phrase in the passage. It summarises the entire act of reflection without showing a moment of it.
   > Cut the summary. Show one specific thought — the first specific thing her mind goes to when she sits down. That one thing will say everything.

---

**Sensory Audit:**

The passage has no sensory grounding at all. It operates entirely at the level of action and abstraction. There is no temperature, no sound, no smell — and this is an opening return-home scene, which should be physically present, because the physical world is what is triggering the memory and the emotion.

*Missing — smell:* Return-home scenes are often triggered by smell (smell is the most direct pathway to memory). The smell of this town — bakery bread, car exhaust, something specific — should be present.

*Missing — sound:* What does the main street sound like at this time of day? What does the bakery sound like? Sound locates the reader.

*Missing — physical sensation:* Temperature, light, the feel of the chair she sits in. She has been away for fifteen years. What does her body register on returning to this air?

*Suggested sensory addition (smell + sound):* When she passes the bakery, the smell of the bread stops her — not because she expected it, but because she didn't. The body remembered something the mind thought it had let go.

---

**Single Main Weakness:**

The prose is operating entirely in summary mode — it tells us what Nadia did and felt without ever placing us inside a specific, physical moment. Every line is at the same distance: reported rather than experienced.

---

**Rewritten Sample Passage:**

*Nadia returned to the town where she had grown up after fifteen years away. It looked smaller than she remembered — or she had just gotten larger, she supposed, and the ratio had shifted.*

*The main street was mostly the same. A phone shop where the hardware store had been. The bakery at the corner with the same hand-painted sign, slightly more faded. She was almost past it before the smell reached her: bread and something underneath it, slightly sweet, yeast and warmth. Her body stopped moving before she decided to stop. Fifteen years, and her feet still knew.*

*She ordered a coffee at the counter and took it outside. The sun was low and cold on her face. She wrapped her hands around the cup and sat with it and didn't look at the end of the street where her parents' house was. She had twenty minutes before she had to. She would use them.*

**Changes applied:**
- "felt strange" → removed; strangeness delivered through the body stopping involuntarily at the bakery smell
- "complicated feelings" → removed; the final lines ("didn't look at the end of the street," "she had twenty minutes before she had to") give the complication without naming it
- "got a coffee" → "ordered a coffee at the counter" — active verb, placed in space
- "looked at things" → replaced with specific details (phone shop / hardware store, the bakery sign) and a specific avoidance (not looking at the end of the street)
- Sensory addition: smell (bread, yeast) + temperature (sun, cold) + touch (hands around the cup)
- "thought about her life" → cut; replaced with the specific thought that says everything: she has twenty minutes before she has to look at the house
