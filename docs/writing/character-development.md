# `/s4h-writing-character-development`

> *Flat characters come from describing traits. Compelling characters come from engineering contradictions.*

**Category:** [Writing](README.md) · **Command:** `/s4h-writing-character-development`

---

## What it does

Maps the psychological architecture of a character — want vs. need, wound, defence mechanism, and defining contradiction — to produce a profile that generates specific, unpredictable, recognisable behaviour on the page. The output is not a description of who the character is; it is the mechanism that produces who they are.

---

## When to use it

- A character feels flat, generic, or interchangeable with others
- A character's behaviour feels inconsistent or unmotivated
- You're starting a new character and want psychological depth from the beginning
- A character's arc isn't working — they change but the change doesn't feel earned
- You can describe the character in adjectives but can't predict how they'd behave in a new scene

---

## When *not* to use it

**Don't use this for characters who are deliberately archetypal.** Some stories benefit from characters who function as roles rather than psychological individuals — the mentor, the threshold guardian, the trickster. Applying deep psychological architecture to figures who are meant to serve a structural function can overweight them and pull the story off-centre.

**Don't use this as a character biography tool.** This skill is not about backstory, timeline, or life history — it is about the psychological mechanism that produces behaviour. A character profile with a rich backstory but no wound-and-defence structure will still produce flat characters on the page. The point is the *engine*, not the history.

The nearest neighbor: [`/s4h-writing-arc-design`](arc-design.md) — which addresses how the character's internal change maps to the plot's external structure. Use character-development to build the character profile; use arc-design to map how that psychology is transformed by the story's events.

---

## Full example

**Input:**
> My fantasy novel has a protagonist — a young woman named Senna who was raised by warrior monks after her village was destroyed when she was six. She's described as brave and determined. Readers say she feels flat and hard to connect with. I want to make her feel psychologically real.

---

**Output:**

### Character Profile: Senna

**Core Want:** Senna wants to be the warrior who prevents what happened to her village from happening to anyone else. Specifically: she wants to be powerful enough that she never has to stand helpless while people she loves are destroyed. She defines her life's value through this mission.

**Core Need:** Senna needs to learn that protecting people requires letting them matter to her — that armour, including the warrior identity she has built as armour, is incompatible with the intimacy that makes protection meaningful. She needs to be vulnerable enough to actually be in relationship with the people she is protecting. Right now, she protects abstractions. She needs to protect *people*.

**The Wound:** At six years old, Senna was hidden and survived while her family and village were killed. She did not fight. She could not fight. She hid, and she lived, and everyone else died. The wound installed this belief: *survival through powerlessness is a form of complicity.* To be safe is to be guilty. The only way to live without that guilt is to become the person who would not have hidden — the person who would have fought, even uselessly, even fatally. The warrior monks gave her the form for this belief to inhabit.

**Defence Mechanism: Controlled Competence**
Senna manages the wound through performance of capability. She trains harder than anyone. She volunteers for the most dangerous assignments. She refuses help, declines comfort, and treats vulnerability in others with a brisk efficiency that reads as contempt but is actually terror. Her defence is: *if I am skilled enough, if I am useful enough, if I am not reliant on anyone, then I can never again be the person who hid.* She keeps people at exactly the distance where they are useful to her mission and cannot be lost from her.

The defence has a tell: when someone expresses care for her, Senna redirects immediately to task. "You did well out there, are you hurt?" — she fields the concern and returns to operational assessment before it can land.

**Story Relationship:** Senna is precisely unequipped to handle a story that requires her to trust another person with a piece of the mission, or to make a decision between her mission and a specific person she loves. Her defence mechanism — controlled competence, task-orientation, pre-emptive emotional withdrawal — is the exact wrong tool for any story that requires her to be in genuine relationship. She can save the world abstractly, but not if it requires risking losing someone specific.

**Arc Endpoint:** Senna must arrive at the belief: *I am allowed to love specific people and still fight for them.* The wound told her that attachment leads to helplessness. The arc must show her that attachment is actually the only thing that makes the fight meaningful — and that the person who hid at six years old was a child, not a coward, and has been punishing herself for three decades for something that was not her fault.

**Defining Contradiction:** Genuinely devoted to protecting people; structurally incapable of letting anyone be close enough to actually be protected by her.

**Behavioural Tells:**
1. When someone is injured, Senna becomes more efficient, not more tender — she assesses, treats, and moves on without asking how the person feels, only whether they can continue.
2. When complimented on her fighting, she immediately names what she did wrong: "I left my left side open on the second engagement." It is not false modesty — she genuinely cannot receive praise because receiving it would mean she is enough, and she cannot afford to believe that.
3. She knows the name and fighting style of every monk she trains with, but not their families, their histories, their fears. She has only the information she needs to fight alongside them.
4. She volunteers for solo assignments with a quiet certainty that reads as confidence and is actually relief: alone, she cannot be the reason someone dies.
5. When someone in her company is captured, she spends the rescue-planning phase in a state of controlled intensity — faster, sharper, no excess movement — that her colleagues read as focus and that is actually the only time she feels legitimate: she is useful, she is fighting, she is not hiding.
