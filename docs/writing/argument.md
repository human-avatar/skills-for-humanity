# `/s4h-writing-argument`

> *Arguments fail not in the evidence but in the warrant — the unstated principle that connects them.*

**Category:** [Writing](README.md) · **Command:** `/s4h-writing-argument`

---

## What it does

Builds and repairs persuasive arguments by surfacing the warrant, auditing evidence quality, identifying the strongest counterargument and whether it is addressed, and flagging rhetorical substitutes that are standing in for reasoning. Produces the argument rebuilt at full strength.

---

## When to use it

- An op-ed, essay, or brief isn't convincing people who should be persuadable
- An argument has strong anecdotal evidence but the logical case isn't landing
- You're preparing a case to make to leadership, a board, or a skeptical audience
- An argument has been challenged and you need to understand where it's weak
- You're writing persuasive content and want to build it on a logically sound foundation
- A colleague says "I can see you believe this, but I'm not convinced" and you can't identify why

---

## When *not* to use it

**Don't use this to win an argument you shouldn't win.** This skill builds the strongest version of an argument. If the strongest version is weak — if the evidence genuinely doesn't support the claim, or the warrant is genuinely contestable — the skill will surface that. The output is an honest assessment of argument strength, not a rhetorical amplifier.

**Don't use this when the problem is emotional, not logical.** Some resistance to arguments is not a reasoning problem — it is a values difference or a relationship problem. A person who disagrees with your values about what matters will not be persuaded by a stronger logical argument. In those cases, understanding the values difference is more useful than perfecting the argument structure.

The nearest neighbor: [`/s4h-writing-rhetoric`](rhetoric.md) — which analyses how an argument is being made rhetorically (its appeals, its frames, its assumptions). Argument analysis asks "is the logic sound?"; rhetoric analysis asks "what is the text doing?" Use argument for logical assessment; use rhetoric for the full rhetorical picture including things that argument analysis doesn't catch.

---

## Full example

**Input:**
> I'm writing an op-ed arguing for a 4-day work week in our industry. My evidence is: (1) three anecdotes from companies that tried it and reported success, (2) my own team's experience during a summer pilot where productivity "felt the same," (3) a general claim that "studies show" workers are more productive when well-rested. My editor says the argument has holes.

---

**Output:**

### Argument Audit

**Central Claim:** The industry should adopt a 4-day work week.

---

**Warrant — Stated Explicitly:**

The connecting principle in this argument is: *if workers maintain the same output in 4 days that they currently produce in 5, then reducing working hours to 4 days costs the organisation nothing and benefits workers.* There is a secondary implied warrant: *policies that benefit workers without costing organisations should be adopted.*

Both warrants need to be examined.

**Warrant 1 is contestable:** "Maintains the same output" assumes output is the right measure, that output is measurable, and that short-term pilot results generalise to sustained conditions. A skeptical reader will ask: does productivity remain equal in week 40 of a 4-day week, not just during an enthusiastic pilot? What happens to client-facing responsiveness? What about roles where coverage matters?

**Warrant 2 is substantially contestable:** The argument assumes that "benefits workers without costing organisations" is sufficient reason to adopt a policy. A reader who believes "organisations should maintain competitive advantage" may accept the evidence and reject the warrant — "so what if our workers prefer it? What do our competitors do? What happens to client expectations set by 5-day firms?"

The argument has two warrants, both of which are doing heavy work and neither of which is addressed in the argument. The editor's "holes" are here.

---

**Evidence Audit:**

**Evidence 1 — Three company anecdotes:**
- Sufficient? No. Three anecdotes from self-selected companies (who volunteered their success stories) are not a representative sample. Companies that tried the 4-day week and returned to 5 are not represented.
- Credible? Moderate. Named companies with documented cases are more credible than unnamed ones. Are these companies named? Are the results specific?
- Relevant? Yes — if they are companies of comparable size, industry, and structure to your target audience.

Diagnosis: Insufficient as the primary evidence. Anecdotes support a broader evidence base; they cannot substitute for one. Use them as illustration, not as the main case.

**Evidence 2 — Own team's pilot:**
- Sufficient? No. One team, one pilot, subjective self-assessment ("felt the same").
- Credible? Low. Self-reported productivity during a motivated pilot is not a reliable measure. The Hawthorne effect (people perform better when they know they're being observed/evaluated) is significant here.
- Relevant? Yes — but needs objective measures, not subjective ones.

Diagnosis: As stated, this evidence undermines the argument by calling attention to its subjectivity. "Felt the same" will be dismissed by a skeptical reader. Replace with objective measures if they exist; if they don't, don't use it as evidence.

**Evidence 3 — "Studies show" workers are more productive when well-rested:**
- Sufficient? Potentially, if the studies are named.
- Credible? Currently near-zero. "Studies show" without citing the studies is not evidence — it is the claim that evidence exists. Any informed reader will ask: which studies? What did they actually measure? Are they replicated?
- Relevant? The claim is about rest and productivity, not specifically about 4-day work weeks. The connection needs to be made explicitly.

Diagnosis: Replace "studies show" with specific studies, specific findings, and specific applicability to the 4-day week context. If specific studies don't exist or don't say what you've implied they say, remove this evidence entirely.

---

**Strongest Counterargument:**

The strongest objection to this argument: *the 4-day work week may work for knowledge work roles in internally-focused teams, but it creates structural disadvantage in client-facing, deadline-driven, or competitive markets where availability matters.*

Specifically: if your clients work 5 days and your competitors work 5 days, a 4-day shop is 20% less available. The productivity claim doesn't address availability — a team that produces the same output in 4 days but is unreachable on Fridays is not the same as a 5-day team for the client.

**Addressed in current argument?** No. This is the most powerful objection and the argument currently ignores it. A skeptical reader raises this objection immediately and, finding no response, discounts the whole.

**What an honest response looks like:** Acknowledge the availability concern directly. Show that the companies cited addressed it (staggered schedules, rotating coverage, client communication) and that the feared client response did not materialise. Or acknowledge that the 4-day model works in some contexts but not all, and scope your claim accordingly.

---

**Rhetorical Substitutes:**

1. "Studies show workers are more productive when well-rested" — this is an appeal to unnamed authority. It sounds like evidence but is the assertion that evidence exists, not the evidence itself.

2. "Productivity felt the same" — self-referential authority; appeals to personal experience in a way that reads as subjective to anyone outside the experience.

3. The implicit structure of the argument moves from "some companies tried this and liked it" to "we should all do it" — a rhetorical leap by accumulation (three examples stand in for a systematic case).

None of these are illegitimate rhetorical tools per se, but all three are currently substituting for the logical substance of the case rather than supporting it.

---

**Verdict on Argument Strength:** Weak as currently structured. The anecdotes are illustrations without an evidence base. The warrant is unstated and contestable. The strongest counterargument is not addressed. The argument will convince people who already agree and will be discounted by anyone who doesn't.

---

**Reconstruction at Full Strength:**

*Claim:* Knowledge work organisations in our industry should pilot a 4-day work week with the goal of sustainable adoption.

*Warrant made explicit:* When output is maintained and availability can be managed, organisations should prefer policies that benefit employee wellbeing — because wellbeing is correlated with retention, and retention reduces the talent acquisition costs that currently represent our industry's largest recurring expense.

*Evidence:*
- [Specific named studies on productivity and working hours — replace "studies show" with the actual studies]
- [Named company case studies with objective output measures, not just self-reported success, and including companies of comparable size and structure]
- [Address the Hawthorne effect in own pilot: if no objective measures exist, remove this evidence]

*Counterargument addressed:* The availability concern is real and specific. The strongest 4-day implementations address it through staggered coverage, not by pretending Fridays don't matter. [Name specific implementation approaches from the case study companies.]

*Conclusion:* The case is strongest for knowledge work roles in internally-focused teams, weakest for client-facing roles in competitive markets where Friday availability matters. Scope the recommendation accordingly: pilot in the right roles before extending.
