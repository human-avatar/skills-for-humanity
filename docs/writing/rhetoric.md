# `/writing-rhetoric`

> *Rhetoric asks not what a piece says, but what it is doing. The frame often does more work than the argument.*

**Category:** [Writing](README.md) · **Command:** `/writing-rhetoric`

---

## What it does

Analyses the rhetorical situation of a piece of writing — speaker, audience, purpose, context — then surfaces the explicit argument vs. the rhetorical frame (what is claimed vs. what is assumed), maps the appeals (logos, ethos, pathos), and flags loaded language carrying ideological weight without acknowledgment.

---

## When to use it

- Analysing a political speech, policy document, op-ed, or persuasive piece
- You sense a document is doing something beyond what it explicitly argues but can't name it
- Evaluating the rhetoric of a competitor, opponent, or organisation
- Preparing a counter-argument and need to understand what you're actually responding to
- Checking your own writing for assumptions you haven't surfaced
- Academic analysis of persuasion, propaganda, or institutional communication

---

## When *not* to use it

**Don't use this to dismiss arguments by calling them "rhetoric."** Rhetoric analysis is descriptive, not automatically pejorative. All effective communication involves rhetorical choices. The question this skill answers is whether the rhetorical choices are honest and proportionate — whether they serve the argument or substitute for it. A finding that a speech uses pathos is not a finding that the speech is wrong.

**Don't use this as a substitute for argument analysis.** Rhetoric analysis surfaces what a piece is doing; argument analysis evaluates whether the logic is sound. A rhetorically sophisticated piece may also be logically valid, and a rhetorically clumsy piece may be entirely correct. For the logical assessment, use [`/writing-argument`](argument.md). These two skills are complementary, not redundant.

The nearest neighbor: [`/writing-argument`](argument.md) — which asks "is the argument sound?" while rhetoric asks "what is the argument doing?" Use rhetoric first to surface the frame and identify what is being argued implicitly; then use argument to evaluate the explicit case. In combination they give the fullest picture.

---

## Full example

**Input:**
> Analyse this excerpt from a political speech:
>
> "We stand at a crossroads. Our parents and grandparents built this nation through sacrifice and hard work. Now, those who would open our borders and erase our culture threaten to give away everything they worked for. We must protect our future — for our children, for their children, and for the values that have always made this country great. The choice is clear: stand with those who built this nation, or surrender it to strangers."

---

**Output:**

### Rhetorical Analysis

**Rhetorical Situation:**

- **Speaker:** A political figure speaking in the first-person plural ("we stand," "our parents"), constructing themselves as representative of a collective rather than as an individual.
- **Audience:** The intended audience is constructed within the speech itself — defined as people who share the "we" identity (those whose grandparents built the nation, those who have a culture to protect, those with children to protect the future for). The speech defines its audience by implication: those not in this "we" are "strangers" or "those who would" threaten it.
- **Purpose:** Mobilisation. This is not primarily an informational speech; it is a call to action ("stand with those who built this nation") framed as a binary choice.
- **Context:** Electoral or political campaign context; likely addressing a crowd of supporters. The "crossroads" framing signals this is a moment-of-choice speech, not a policy speech.

---

**Explicit Argument vs. Rhetorical Frame:**

**Explicit argument:** There are people who want to "open borders" and "erase our culture," and we must oppose them to protect what previous generations built.

**Rhetorical frame (what is assumed without stating):**

1. **"We" is unified and coherent.** The speech assumes a unified "we" — "our parents and grandparents," "our culture," "our children." This "we" is never defined. Who counts? The speech allows listeners to include themselves in the "we" while excluding others from it, without naming the criterion of inclusion. The "we" is constructed by the speech, not described by it.

2. **Culture is singular and preservable.** "Our culture" is presented as a stable, definable entity that can be "erased." The frame assumes culture is not continuously changing, not already multiple, not a product of continuous immigration and mixing. This assumption is doing significant work and is never examined.

3. **Immigration is framed as taking.** "Give away everything they worked for" frames immigration as a zero-sum transfer of something owned. The alternative frame — immigration as a contribution to something shared — is absent. The "give away" metaphor encodes a specific economic model of national wealth without naming it.

4. **The binary is the only frame.** "Stand with those who built this nation, or surrender it to strangers" presents exactly two options. The speech suppresses a third frame: that policy can both respond to border concerns and acknowledge the contributions of immigration. The binary is rhetorical, not logical.

5. **"Always" erases historical complexity.** "Values that have always made this country great" implies an unchanging value set with an unbroken history of greatness. The "always" forecloses examination of historical periods that don't fit — not by arguing against them, but by constructing a reality in which they don't exist.

---

**Appeals Map:**

**Logos (Reasoning):** Near-absent. There is no specific claim supported by specific evidence. No policy is named, no outcome is projected, no mechanism is described. The logical structure is: threat exists → we must respond → binary choice. The threat is unnamed (who specifically? what specific policy?) and the response is unspecified (what should actually happen?). The reasoning is skeletal — a frame with no content.

**Ethos (Authority/Credibility):** The speaker's authority is established by inclusion in the "we." They do not claim personal expertise or institutional authority; they claim identity with the audience. "We stand" rather than "I believe." The credibility appeal is to shared membership, not to knowledge or achievement.

**Pathos (Emotion):** Dominant. The speech operates almost entirely on emotional appeals: generational reverence ("parents and grandparents"), fear ("threaten to give away"), family love ("our children, their children"), and pride ("always made this country great"). The emotional density is high — the speech is designed to produce a felt experience of threat and solidarity, not to inform or reason.

**Dominant appeal:** Pathos, by a significant margin. The emotional appeals are not supporting a logical case — they are the case. This is the core rhetorical problem: the speech substitutes emotional mobilisation for argument.

---

**Loaded Language:**

1. **"Open our borders"** — Frame encoded: borders are currently appropriately closed, and "opening" them is an extreme, reckless action. The alternative frame (immigration reform as a managed process) is suppressed by the word "open." Alternative: "change immigration policy" is neutral; "open borders" implies abolishing enforcement entirely, which almost no mainstream position holds.

2. **"Erase our culture"** — Frame encoded: there is a unified, definable culture that can be destroyed by outsiders. The word "erase" implies intentional destruction. Alternative: "change our culture" or "contribute to our culture" are alternative framings of the same demographic change.

3. **"Give away"** — Frame encoded: national resources are property owned by specific people, and immigration policy transfers ownership to others. The transactional metaphor encodes a zero-sum economic model without naming it. Alternative: "invest in" or "share with" are alternative framings.

4. **"Strangers"** — Frame encoded: immigrants and those who support immigration policy are outside the "we" and therefore outside the community of moral concern. The word "strangers" is not hostile (like "invaders") but does the work of exclusion by implication. Alternative: "newcomers," "immigrants," "people seeking entry."

5. **"Always made this country great"** — Frame encoded: the country's values are unchanging and their greatness uninterrupted. "Always" forecloses historical examination. Alternative: "that have defined us at our best" acknowledges both the values and the moments they were not met.

---

**Verdict:**

The rhetoric is disproportionate to the argument it purports to make. The speech contains no actual argument — no named policy, no specific evidence, no mechanism of action. It is a purely rhetorical construct: a collection of emotional appeals, loaded frames, and binary choices that produce a felt experience of threat and belonging without providing any informational content that could be examined, challenged, or evaluated.

This is not rhetoric serving an argument. This is rhetoric substituting for one. The audience who responds positively to this speech will not have been persuaded by logic — they will have been activated by emotion and identity. That is an honest rhetorical assessment of what the speech is doing.

The most significant finding: the binary ("stand with us or surrender") is the speech's closing move and its most important rhetorical act. It is designed to foreclose deliberation by making it shameful. A speech that ends by making independent judgment seem like betrayal is not trying to persuade — it is trying to prevent persuasion from happening.
