# `/writing-scene-construction`

> *Every scene is a mini-story. Without a want, obstacle, and changed outcome, it's not a scene — it's a passage of time.*

**Category:** [Writing](README.md) · **Command:** `/writing-scene-construction`

---

## What it does

Diagnoses individual scenes using the want/obstacle/outcome framework, assesses sensory grounding and subtext, and determines the scene's structural function. Produces a verdict — keep, cut, combine, or rewrite — with specific recommendations. Every scene must change the story's state; this skill identifies those that don't and shows why.

---

## When to use it

- A specific scene isn't working but you can't identify why
- A scene feels static or like nothing is happening
- Beta readers skip or skim a particular section
- You suspect a scene can be cut but aren't sure
- You've written a scene that feels fine but doesn't pull its weight in the story
- A scene has good dialogue or description but still reads as flat

---

## When *not* to use it

**Don't use this for summary passages or transitions.** Not everything in a story is a scene — some passages are deliberate summary (condensed time), transition (movement from one location or time to another), or reflection (a character processing what has happened). These passages don't need a want/obstacle/outcome structure; they serve different functions. Scene construction applies to dramatised moments where the story is in real time.

**Don't use this before fixing the surrounding structure.** A scene might be failing because the structural beat it belongs to is itself failing — because the midpoint hasn't shifted the story's direction, a scene within that section has no function to perform. Fix the structure first; the scene's problems may resolve.

The nearest neighbor: [`/writing-dialogue`](dialogue.md) — which addresses what happens within the scene when the core scene architecture is correct but the conversation inside it isn't working. If the scene has a clear goal, obstacle, and outcome but still reads as flat, the problem is likely in the dialogue; use that skill next.

---

## Full example

**Input:**
> My detective novel has an interrogation scene between Detective Marquez and suspect Leon, who she believes killed his business partner. The scene is in Chapter 8, just after Marquez gets the warrant. Leon is cooperative, answers all questions directly, and the scene ends with Marquez not getting any new information. It feels flat. Here's the scene in brief: Marquez arrives at Leon's apartment. Leon invites her in. She asks about the night of the murder; he answers each question calmly and completely. She asks where he was; he has an alibi she can verify. He offers her coffee. She declines. She leaves.

---

**Output:**

### Scene Diagnosis

**Goal:** Marquez's goal in this scene is to get Leon to say something incriminating, or to find a hole in his alibi. This goal is present but it operates entirely on the surface — she asks questions, she gets answers. There is no tactical dimension to her approach, no escalation, no moment where she tries something different.

**Obstacle:** Leon's cooperation is technically an obstacle — cooperative suspects are harder to break than defensive ones — but it is not *active*. Leon is not doing anything. He is simply answering. The scene lacks an antagonist who has his own goal in this encounter.

*Diagnosis: This is the primary structural failure. Every interrogation scene needs two players with opposing goals. Marquez wants information or an incriminating slip. What does Leon want? If he is guilty, he wants to appear cooperative while giving away nothing. If he is innocent, he wants to be helpful but is also scared. Currently Leon has no goal — he is a witness stand, not a character. The scene has no conflict because one player has no stake.*

**Outcome Type:** Unmodified NO — Marquez asks questions, gets no information, leaves. The scene ends in exactly the same state it began: Marquez suspects Leon but can't prove it; Leon has said nothing useful. This is an outcome fail.

*The story's state has not changed. Whatever comes next in Chapter 9 could follow directly from Chapter 7 without this scene — it has added nothing to the situation, the characters' knowledge, or the reader's understanding.*

*Recommended outcome types:*
- *No-and: Marquez leaves with no information AND Leon has learned something about what Marquez suspects, which changes his behaviour from here. He is now alert; she has shown him where the investigation is looking.*
- *No-but: Marquez gets no alibi breach but notices something — a tell, an inconsistency in Leon's manner, an object in the apartment — that opens a new line of investigation. The scene closes one door and opens another.*
- *Yes-but: Marquez gets an alibi that seems solid — but the alibi involves a person who, when verified, introduces a new complication.*

**Sensory Inventory:** The scene as described operates entirely at the level of dialogue and action. No sensory detail is mentioned. This is a significant problem for an interrogation scene, where the physical environment carries enormous meaning: how comfortable Leon is in his own space, the signs of his wealth or stress, the temperature of the interaction.

*Missing: the apartment itself (what does it tell us about Leon?), the physical dynamic between the two of them (does he remain seated when she enters? does she accept or refuse his gesture to sit?), any sensory marker of Leon's state (calm hands, careful not to look at something, the specific coffee he offers).*

*Recommended addition: One specific, surprising sensory detail that tells Marquez something she can't prove — and the reader something about Leon. Example: the apartment is very clean. Not tidy — clean, in the way places are cleaned by someone who knows what forensics looks for.*

**Subtext Present:** None currently. Every line of the scene is surface — Marquez asks, Leon answers, both say exactly what they mean. The scene has no subtext because it has no stakes beneath the surface exchange.

*What the scene should carry in subtext:*
- *Marquez knows he's lying (or suspects it so strongly it amounts to the same thing). She's not asking questions to get answers — she's asking questions to watch how he manages the pressure of knowing she doesn't believe him.*
- *Leon knows she doesn't believe him. His cooperation is a performance — but a performance of what? Innocence? Reasonable civic cooperation? Or just competent guilt management?*

*The scene should be two people playing chess while discussing the weather. Currently they are only discussing the weather.*

**Scene Function:** Currently only TRANSITION — this scene moves Marquez from "got the warrant" to "still has no evidence." It performs no revelation, escalation, or character function. A scene serving only one function is at risk; a scene serving only *transition* is doing the minimum possible.

**Verdict:** Rewrite

**Specific Recommendation:** Rebuild the scene with Leon as an active player with his own goal. Give him a specific objective in this scene: he wants to learn exactly what she knows so he can calibrate his next move. Give the scene a no-but outcome: Marquez leaves without her alibi breach, but something has shifted — either Leon has revealed more than he intended through his precision, or Marquez has realised the cooperation itself is a message. Add one specific sensory detail that operates as pure subtext. The scene should end with both players slightly worse off than they wanted to be.
