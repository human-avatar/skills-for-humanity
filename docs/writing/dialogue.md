# `/s4h-writing-dialogue`

> *Real conversation is about what is not said. The subtext is the scene.*

**Category:** [Writing](README.md) · **Command:** `/s4h-writing-dialogue`

---

## What it does

Analyses dialogue for surface vs. subtext goals, voice differentiation, exposition, scene function, and forward momentum. Produces line-by-line notes on strongest and weakest exchanges, with specific rewrites for flagged lines. Repairs dialogue that says exactly what it means, sounds interchangeable, or explains things characters already know.

---

## When to use it

- Dialogue sounds on-the-nose — characters say exactly what they mean
- Characters sound like variations of the same person
- A scene has good structure but the conversation inside it is flat
- Characters are explaining things to each other that they both already know
- Dialogue advances plot but doesn't feel like real people talking
- A confrontation scene is too direct — the conflict is stated rather than enacted

---

## When *not* to use it

**Don't use this when the scene structure is the problem.** If the scene lacks a goal, obstacle, and outcome, the dialogue inside it will be flat regardless of how good the individual lines are. Fix the scene structure first (see [`/s4h-writing-scene-construction`](scene-construction.md)), then apply dialogue repair.

**Don't use this to impose obliqueness for its own sake.** Subtext means the real stakes are present beneath the surface exchange — it does not mean the dialogue must be cryptic or obscure. Some moments require directness: confession, declaration, ultimatum. Not all on-the-nose dialogue is a failure. The question is whether the directness is earned by the scene's dramatic moment, or whether it is the writer's default mode.

The nearest neighbor: [`/s4h-writing-character-development`](character-development.md) — which reveals why a character speaks the way they do. Voice differentiation problems are almost always character problems: if you don't know what a character can't say directly, can't ask for, can't admit, you don't yet know their voice. Fix the character first; the voice will follow.

---

## Full example

**Input:**
> I have a confrontation scene between two estranged siblings — Mara (36) and Daniel (29). Mara left home at 19 and barely kept in touch. Daniel stayed, took care of their sick mother, and resents Mara for leaving. They meet at the mother's funeral. The problem is everything they say is exactly what they mean — it's too direct and it feels hollow. Here's the scene:
>
> MARA: "I know you're angry at me."
> DANIEL: "You abandoned us. You left me to deal with everything."
> MARA: "I had to leave. You know what that house was like."
> DANIEL: "I know exactly what it was like. I stayed."
> MARA: "I'm sorry. I should have come back more."
> DANIEL: "It's too late to be sorry."

---

**Output:**

### Dialogue Analysis

**Speaker Goals:**

- Mara (surface): To apologise, to re-establish contact, to have the conversation end without more damage.
- Mara (subtext): She wants Daniel to release her from the guilt she has carried for seventeen years. She wants him to tell her that leaving was understandable, that she isn't a terrible person. She is asking for absolution while appearing to be offering an apology.
- Daniel (surface): To communicate the full extent of his grievance.
- Daniel (subtext): He wants her to know what she cost him — specifically, not generally. He wants her to understand the years, not just the fact of them. He is not asking for an apology; he is asking to be witnessed. He doesn't want her sorry. He wants her to know what "sorry" is *for*.

*FLAG: Both characters are speaking their subtext directly. "I know you're angry at me" is Mara naming what she senses; "You abandoned us" is Daniel saying the thing he'd never quite say this plainly. The scene has collapsed its own subtext into its surface text. The result: a confrontation with all the noise of conflict and none of the depth.*

---

**Voice Differentiation:**

Currently both voices are undifferentiated — both speak in complete sentences, both name their emotional states directly, both have the same rhythm. The only difference is their position (accuser vs. apologiser).

- Mara's voice should express her psychology: she is a person who *manages* situations, who has learned to handle difficulty through controlled verbal competence. She should speak in a register that is familiar with this kind of conversation because she has rehearsed it. Her fluency with the emotional language is itself a tell: she has been having this conversation with herself for seventeen years.
- Daniel's voice should express his psychology: he stayed, he did the work, he is not fluent with emotional discourse because he didn't have the luxury of it — he was the one dealing with the reality. His voice should be more concrete, less language-of-feeling, more *what actually happened on which Tuesday*.

---

**Exposition Flags:**

"You abandoned us. You left me to deal with everything." — Both characters already know this. This is the entire history of their relationship. Neither needs to be told. The information is there for the reader's benefit, not the scene's.

"You know what that house was like" / "I know exactly what it was like. I stayed." — This is two characters informing each other of things they both experienced. The exposition problem: shared history cannot be delivered this way without breaking the scene's reality.

---

**Scene Function:** Currently only CONFLICT — a statement and counter-statement of grievances. No revelation, no bonding, no negotiation, no deflection. The scene needs at minimum to generate one revelation: something one character learns about the other that they didn't know.

---

**Forward Momentum:** The scene stalls immediately. The first and last lines are in approximately the same state. Nothing has happened except a statement of the problem. The scene needs to move through at least one shift: a moment where the attack changes, or the defence breaks, or something unexpected surfaces.

---

**Rewrite for Flagged Lines:**

The rewrite moves the subtext underground. Neither character names what they want. Both are talking *about* something else — the logistics of the day, the objects in the house, a specific memory — while the real argument runs beneath.

---

*MARA: "The lilies were wrong. She hated lilies. They were always funeral flowers to her, she said that."*

*DANIEL: "I know. I chose them."*

*A pause.*

*MARA: "Oh."*

*DANIEL: "The chrysanthemums she liked were eighty dollars more. I thought — I thought I'd let the funeral be what it was."*

*MARA: "Daniel —"*

*DANIEL: "They had a good turnover on the lilies. Most popular choice, they said."*

*MARA: "I would have paid."*

*DANIEL: "I know you would have. That's not the point. That's actually not even slightly the point."*

*MARA: "Then what —"*

*DANIEL: "Go look at her house, Mara. Take a proper look. And then come talk to me about flowers."*

---

*In this version: Mara's guilt comes through in her attention to the detail (the lilies, the wrongness). Daniel's grievance comes through in his choices (he chose the wrong flowers deliberately — or in exhaustion, or in a moment of giving up). Neither character names the real conversation. The line "That's not the point. That's actually not even slightly the point" is doing more work than the entire original scene: it tells us that Daniel knows what she's offering and has decided it's not enough — without either of them saying so.*
