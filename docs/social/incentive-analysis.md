# social-incentive-analysis

> Maps the actual incentives driving behavior — distinguishing stated motivations from what the system rewards.

**Trigger:** `/s4h-social-incentive-analysis`

---

## What it does

Identifies what a system actually rewards vs. what it claims to value. Maps how those incentives shape behavior. Explains why people who are trying to do the right thing produce the wrong outcomes when the incentive structure works against them.

---

## When to use

- "Why do they keep doing X when they know Y is better?"
- Stated goals and actual behavior persistently diverge
- A policy change isn't producing the behavior it was designed to produce
- "Follow the incentives"

---

## Example

**Input:**
> Our product team claims to be customer-focused. But features consistently ship with rough edges, customer feedback is collected but rarely acted on, and the roadmap is driven by internal stakeholder requests more than user needs. Why?

**Output:**

**What the system actually rewards:**

*Shipping velocity:* Features shipped is visible. Features done well is hard to measure. Engineers and PMs get credit for launching, not for the quality of what launched. The incentive is completion, not quality.

*Stakeholder satisfaction:* Internal stakeholders (Sales, Marketing, Executives) have proximate relationships with the product team. Their requests are immediate, specific, and come with relationship consequences for non-delivery. Customer needs are mediated through data and are diffuse. The incentive is to respond to the squeaky wheel.

*Appearing responsive:* Collecting customer feedback and noting it in a doc is visible activity. Acting on that feedback — which requires de-prioritizing something else — has a clear cost. The incentive is to collect, not to act.

*Quarterly planning cycles:* A quarter is too short to see the compounding benefit of customer-focused product decisions. It's long enough to see the immediate cost of disappointing an internal stakeholder. The incentive structure has the wrong time horizon.

**Why good intentions aren't enough:**

Every person on the product team probably genuinely wants to build good products. But the incentive structure punishes behavior that prioritizes customers and rewards behavior that prioritizes speed and internal stakeholder satisfaction. In that structure, even well-intentioned people produce the observed behavior. This is not a values problem; it's a measurement and accountability problem.

**What would need to change:**

1. *Measure customer outcomes explicitly:* Activation rate, retention, NPS segmented by feature cohort. Make these as visible as shipped features.
2. *Give customer feedback a seat in prioritization:* If internal stakeholders can add items to the roadmap through influence, customers need a structural equivalent (a defined allocation of roadmap capacity for customer-sourced requests).
3. *Extend the time horizon:* Track feature adoption and customer satisfaction 90 days post-launch, not just at launch.
