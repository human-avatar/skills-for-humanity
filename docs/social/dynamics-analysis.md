# social-dynamics-analysis

> Identifies the group psychology shaping a team or discussion.

**Trigger:** `/s4h-social-dynamics-analysis`

---

## What it does

Maps active group dynamics: groupthink, status dynamics, psychological safety issues, coalition formation, and silence patterns. Each dynamic has a specific manifestation and a different intervention.

---

## When to use

- "Why does this team behave like this?"
- Meetings where the real conversation happens in the hallway, not the room
- Team dysfunction without clear cause
- "Why won't people speak up?"

---

## Example

**Input:**
> In our engineering team's architecture review meetings, the most senior engineer always speaks first and at length. After they speak, everyone agrees. Ideas that were being discussed before the meeting aren't raised in the room. The team lead thinks the discussions are going well. What's happening?

**Output:**

**Primary dynamic: Authority-driven groupthink**

When the most senior person speaks first and authoritatively, they anchor the group's thinking. Everything said afterward is evaluated relative to their position, not on its own merits. People who disagree face a social cost (challenging the senior person publicly) that exceeds the benefit of raising their concern. The result: apparent consensus that masks real dissent.

**Evidence:**
- Senior engineer speaks first: anchor is set before others contribute
- Everyone agrees after: not because they're convinced, but because disagreement is socially costly
- Pre-meeting conversations don't appear in the room: people are filtering based on anticipated reception, not merit

**Secondary dynamic: Psychological safety deficit**

The team lead thinks meetings are going well. This is a common symptom of low psychological safety — the people with power to improve safety are the ones who experience it most. They're not aware of the filtering happening below them.

**What's being lost:**
The most important ideas are the ones that challenge the senior engineer's view — because those are exactly the ones being suppressed. The meeting is optimizing for harmony and producing intellectual monoculture.

**Specific interventions:**

*Structural (highest leverage):* Change the meeting format so the senior engineer speaks last, not first. "Let's hear from everyone else before [name] summarizes." This directly addresses the anchor problem.

*Process:* Introduce a "silent brainstorm" before any verbal discussion — everyone writes their view before anyone speaks. This removes the anchor before it's set.

*Norm-setting:* The team lead needs to model disagreement publicly — disagree with the senior engineer in a meeting, explicitly. This creates permission for others to do the same.

*Naming the pattern:* Have the senior engineer read this analysis. Many people in their position don't realize they're creating the dynamic. They may be willing to change their behavior if they understand its effect.

**For the team lead:**
The fact that you think meetings are going well is itself important information. The best indicator of psychological safety is whether you hear things in meetings that make you uncomfortable. If meetings are comfortable, the filtering is probably happening.
