# 📖 Narrative

> *Information informs. Story moves.*

---

## What this category is

Narrative tools apply story architecture and framing theory to any communication — a presentation, a strategy document, a pitch, a difficult message. They work on the gap between content that is technically correct and communication that actually changes minds, creates alignment, or moves people to action.

The core insight: how something is framed determines what people can see. The same facts in a different frame produce a different response. These tools make that frame visible and give you tools to change it.

---

## When to reach for it

- Something you're communicating is technically correct but not landing
- A presentation or proposal has good content but feels flat or forgettable
- You want to understand why an audience isn't responding — is it the message or the frame?
- The same situation keeps being interpreted in a way that blocks the outcome you need
- You're structuring a complex communication and want it to move rather than just inform
- You need to create or find the tension that makes people pay attention

---

## When *not* to reach for it

**Don't confuse narrative with spin.** Framing is about revealing truth more effectively, not obscuring it. If the narrative work is making something that's wrong seem right, stop and use [Ethics](../ethics/) or [Identity](../identity/).

**Don't use narrative tools when the real problem is a content problem.** Story architecture can't save a presentation that has no substance. Fix the content first; then structure it.

The nearest neighbor: [Communication](../communication/) — which focuses on audience fit, message clarity, and channel selection. Narrative focuses on story structure, framing, and what creates attention and movement.
