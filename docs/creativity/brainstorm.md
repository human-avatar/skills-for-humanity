# creativity-brainstorm

> Orchestrated multi-method creative thinking session — selects and sequences the right tools for your specific challenge.

**Trigger:** `/s4h-creativity-brainstorm`

---

## What it does

Rather than applying a single technique, this skill diagnoses your thinking challenge and sequences 2–4 methods that build on each other. It closes with a synthesis that produces insights no single method would show alone.

This is the **entry point for serious creative work** — use it when you have a real challenge and want the full toolkit applied, not when you want a single quick technique.

---

## When to use

- "I need to think through this properly"
- "Help me with a hard problem" — without knowing which specific tool fits
- Big decision or challenge where you want comprehensive exploration
- The problem has resisted your usual thinking approaches
- You want to walk away with a clear direction, not just more options

## When *not* to use

- You already know which technique you need → use the specific skill directly
- You need a quick gut check → [plus-minus-interesting](plus-minus-interesting.md) is faster
- The problem is logical or ethical rather than creative → [Logic](../logic/) or [Ethics](../ethics/)

## Example

**Input:**
> We're a 12-person SaaS company. We've been growing 8% MoM for two years. Growth has plateaued at 3% for four months. We've tried increasing ad spend, refreshing onboarding, and a referral program. Nothing is moving the needle. I don't know if this is a product problem, a market problem, or a go-to-market problem. Help me think through it.

**Output:**

**Reading the situation:**
The plateau despite three different interventions (distribution, activation, and referral) suggests the problem isn't any one channel — it's either (a) the assumption that more of the same will work, or (b) something structural about product-market fit at this scale. Starting with assumption-excavator to check the framing, then lateral thinking to escape the dominant "growth tactics" frame.

---

## Assumption Excavator

*What assumptions are buried in how this problem is being framed?*

**Assumption 1: The plateau is the problem.**
What if the plateau is a signal, not the problem? The interventions tried (ads, onboarding, referral) all assume the product is right and acquisition/activation is wrong. What if the product is hitting the edge of the market it fits?

**Assumption 2: 8% MoM growth was sustainable.**
What if the prior growth rate was driven by factors that have naturally exhausted themselves — early adopters, a specific channel with limited reach, or an initial wedge market that's now saturated?

**Assumption 3: The three categories (product/market/GTM) are separable.**
What if this is a positioning problem that looks like all three? A product that was positioned for one buyer type in year one, now being sold to a different buyer type who experiences it differently?

**Load-bearing assumption to test first:** The market isn't saturated. If it is, no amount of GTM improvement will restore 8% MoM growth.

---

## Lateral Thinking

*What's the dominant idea keeping you in one direction?*

Dominant idea: "We need to find the broken channel and fix it." All interventions so far have been channel interventions.

**Escaping the dominant idea — directions that don't involve fixing a channel:**

1. **Redefine who the product is for.** The 8% cohort had a specific profile. What if the 3% cohort is a different profile that the product serves less well? Segment the churned users from the last 4 months vs. the cohort that stayed.

2. **Find the users who are still growing.** Somewhere in the customer base, there are users referring aggressively. What's different about them? Build more of them.

3. **Look at the product ceiling.** Is there a feature or capability that, if it existed, would make users immediately recommend it? Not "what's broken" — what would make it genuinely remarkable?

4. **Question the business model, not the product.** Could the current model (pricing, seat structure, billing cadence) be creating friction that a different model would remove?

---

## Synthesis

**What this session revealed:**

The three interventions tried share a hidden assumption: the product is right and the funnel is the problem. The assumption excavator suggests testing the opposite before optimizing the funnel further. The lateral thinking session surfaced that the existing customer base contains the answer — the users who ARE growing fast know something about the product that isn't being used.

**Most important direction:**
Segment the customer base by growth rate (users who refer and expand vs. users who plateau) and find what's structurally different about the high-growth cohort. This costs nothing to test and answers the product/market/GTM question definitively.

**Recommended next step:**
Pull the 20 accounts that have grown the most in the last 4 months. Interview them this week. Ask: what made them start, what made them stay, what would make them refer. The pattern in those answers is the lever.
