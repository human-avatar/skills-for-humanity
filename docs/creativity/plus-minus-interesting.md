# creativity-plus-minus-interesting

> Evaluates any idea fairly — positives, negatives, and what's worth noticing — before accepting or rejecting it.

**Trigger:** `/s4h-creativity-plus-minus-interesting`

---

## What it does

Applies de Bono's PMI tool to produce a balanced evaluation of any idea, proposal, plan, or decision. Forces genuine consideration of all three categories — including the "Interesting" category, which captures what's noteworthy regardless of valence. The antidote to snap judgment.

---

## When to use

- About to accept or reject an idea quickly — want to check the snap judgment
- Evaluating a specific proposal fairly, especially one you already have a view on
- Want to surface non-obvious upsides or downsides
- "What are the pros and cons of X?" requests

## When *not* to use

- You need multi-perspective analysis across different stakeholder views → [six-hats](six-hats.md)
- You need to compare multiple options → [criteria-weighting](../decision/criteria-weighting.md)

---

## Example

**Input:**
> We're considering moving to a 4-day work week. Run a PMI on it.

**Output:**

**➕ Plus**
- Recruiting and retention signal: differentiates as employer before a candidate even interviews
- Forces prioritization — teams with a hard constraint on time cut low-value work faster than teams with flexibility
- Evidence from pilots at other companies shows productivity maintained or improved in knowledge work
- Reduces burnout-driven attrition, which is expensive
- Increases recovery time, which tends to improve quality of work on the remaining days
- Psychological ownership — employees who trust the company tend to perform better

**➖ Minus**
- Customer-facing functions may struggle — a 4-day week at a 5-day company creates coverage gaps
- Coordination overhead with vendors, partners, and customers who operate on a 5-day week
- Some roles (sales, support, on-call) may require coverage structures that don't fit cleanly
- Risk of "4-day workload crammed into 4 days" — productivity maintained by intensity, not efficiency
- Reversing it later if it doesn't work is harder than it looks (culture expectations will be set)
- Managers who don't trust their teams will police it in ways that undermine the benefit

**💡 Interesting**
- This is as much a management philosophy test as a work-schedule test — companies that can't make it work may have trust or measurement problems worth knowing about
- The day that gets removed matters: Friday vs. Monday vs. Wednesday have different effects on meeting culture and deep work patterns
- It may accelerate the existing trend toward async-first work as a side effect
- The companies where this doesn't work often discover *why* it doesn't work — and that information is valuable regardless of what they decide

**Assessment:**
The Plus column is genuine. The Minus column is mostly implementation challenges, not structural objections — most are solvable with the right design (which day, which roles, what coverage model). The most important factor is whether the management culture can handle it; that's the variable that determines whether this is a win or a failed experiment.
