# creativity-provocation

> Uses a deliberately impossible or absurd statement as a springboard to non-obvious ideas.

**Trigger:** `/s4h-creativity-provocation` or prefix any statement with `Po:`

---

## What it does

Applies de Bono's Provocation Operation (Po): states something obviously impossible or wrong, refuses to evaluate it, and uses it as a departure point. The provocation is not the idea — it's the scaffold. Ideas generated from an impossible premise are then tested for real-world applicability.

---

## When to use

- "Let's try something radical"
- Stuck in conventional thinking and need a deliberate jolt
- Brainstorming has been too conservative — want something genuinely different
- A problem needs an unconventional jolt to break open
- User prefixes a statement with "Po:"

## When *not* to use

- You need to evaluate a real idea → [plus-minus-interesting](plus-minus-interesting.md)
- You need options without the jolt → [alternatives](alternatives.md)

---

## Example

**Input:**
> Po: Customers should have to pay more the less they use the product.

**Output:**

*Not evaluating the provocation — using it as a movement tool.*

**What does the world look like if this were true?**

If heavy users paid less and light users paid more: the pricing signal would be "this product is for people who use it seriously, not casually." Light users would either become heavy users or leave. The customer base would concentrate toward highly engaged users. Support costs would drop (power users self-serve). Churn would drop (the casual users who leave after 3 months would either commit or not start).

**What would you do in this world?**

You'd need to measure usage with precision and create a clear, fair metric for "use." You'd need to communicate the pricing philosophy as a feature, not a punishment — "we're built for people who are serious about X." You'd probably create an onboarding program to help light users become heavy users before their first renewal.

**Which of these ideas work in the real world?**

1. **Usage-tiered pricing in reverse (progressive pricing):** Not backwards, but heavily discounting for high usage rather than low. Large enterprise deals often work this way — per-seat cost drops as seats increase. Same principle, different direction.

2. **"Commitment tier" pricing:** Offer a lower price to customers who commit to a usage minimum. "If you use less than X, you pay more; if you use more than X, you pay the lower rate." Creates incentive to engage.

3. **Onboarding investment for low-usage accounts:** Instead of billing light users more, invest in them — proactive outreach, success programs — to convert them before they churn. The provocation surfaced that light users are high-risk; the real-world idea is to address that risk before it becomes a billing problem.

4. **A product designed *for* heavy users from the start:** The provocation reveals an implicit assumption that the product should serve all users equally. What if it was explicitly designed for power users, with a simpler free tier for casual ones?

**Most promising:** #3 and #4. The provocation revealed that light users are a risk that most products try to retain rather than qualify. Designing explicitly for the customer profile that makes the business work is a real strategic direction.
