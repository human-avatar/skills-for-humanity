# creativity-random-entry

> Breaks cognitive fixation by introducing a random, unrelated stimulus — the randomness is the point.

**Trigger:** `/s4h-creativity-random-entry`

---

## What it does

Applies de Bono's Random Entry technique: selects a random word, object, or image and forces connections between it and the problem. The randomness bypasses the grooves of familiar thinking by starting from a point that has no relationship to the problem — and therefore carries none of its assumptions.

---

## When to use

- "I've exhausted my usual thinking on this"
- Stuck and need inspiration from outside the problem space
- Brainstorming has circled back to the same ideas repeatedly
- "Give me a random creative prompt"
- Need to approach something from a completely fresh angle

## When *not* to use

- Need structured option generation → [alternatives](alternatives.md)
- Have a dominant idea to escape → [lateral-thinking](lateral-thinking.md)

---

## Example

**Input:**
> We're designing a new pricing page. We've tried multiple versions and they all feel generic. Random stimulus please.

**Random word selected:** *Lighthouse*

**Output:**

**Forced connections — lighthouse to pricing page:**

A lighthouse doesn't charge per visit. It's always on. Its purpose is to prevent the worst outcome (shipwreck), not to facilitate a transaction. It's a landmark — you know it's there even when you don't need it. It's built for the worst conditions, not the best.

**Ideas from the connections:**

1. **Always-on safety messaging:** Most pricing pages lead with value ("what you get"). What if this one led with risk reduction — "what you avoid"? For some products, the fear of the bad outcome (data loss, compliance failure, missed opportunities) is stronger than the hope of the good one.

2. **The landmark approach:** What if the pricing page was designed to be remembered, not just read? A distinctive visual element — something as unmistakable as a lighthouse beam — that sticks in memory when someone visits and leaves, so when they come back, they feel orientation rather than confusion.

3. **Built for the storm:** Most pricing pages are designed for a calm, considered visit. What if this one was designed for the moment of maximum stress — someone who just hit a crisis and needs to understand what they're getting *right now*? Ruthlessly clear, no distractions, decision in under 30 seconds.

4. **No charge per "flash":** Lighthouse pricing — one price, everything included, no metering. If the current model is usage-based and confusing, "lighthouse pricing" (flat, always-on, no surprises) could be the differentiator.

**Most promising:** Idea 3 — designing for the stress case. Most pricing pages are optimized for a leisurely, rational evaluator. Designing for someone under pressure (competitive deadline, budget review, boss asking for a recommendation by EOD) would produce something more decisive and clear than optimizing for the calm browsing case.
