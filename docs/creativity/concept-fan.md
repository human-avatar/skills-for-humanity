# creativity-concept-fan

> Expands the solution space at three levels of abstraction — immediate solutions, underlying concepts, and core purpose.

**Trigger:** `/s4h-creativity-concept-fan`

---

## What it does

Applies de Bono's Concept Fan to prevent solution lock-in. Maps options at three levels: (1) the immediate solution being considered, (2) the broader concept it serves, and (3) the underlying purpose behind that concept. Each level generates multiple options, creating a fan of possibilities from tactical to strategic.

---

## When to use

- Locked into one solution type and want to see the full range of approaches
- "Are there ways to achieve this that I haven't considered?"
- Solutions keep failing because they're addressing the wrong level
- About to commit to an implementation before examining whether the approach is right

## When *not* to use

- Need raw options fast without structure → [alternatives](alternatives.md)
- The problem is the framing itself → [assumption-excavator](assumption-excavator.md)

---

## Example

**Input:**
> We want to reduce churn. We've been focused on improving the product's feature set based on exit survey feedback.

**Output:**

**Level 1 — Immediate solutions (what we've been doing)**
- Add features requested in exit surveys
- Fix bugs mentioned in exit feedback
- Improve performance in areas users flagged

**Level 2 — Concepts (what these solutions serve)**
The immediate solutions all serve: *give churning customers what they asked for.*

But there are other concepts that could serve the same goal (reduce churn):
- Identify churning customers before they churn and intervene
- Reduce the switching cost so customers don't churn even when dissatisfied
- Create a community/investment around the product that makes leaving costly
- Change the pricing model so churn has lower financial impact (annual plans vs. monthly)
- Find customers who are deeply satisfied and create more of them, rather than fixing the ones who leave

**Level 3 — Purpose (what the concept serves)**
The underlying purpose: *retain revenue and relationships.*

At the purpose level, entirely different approaches open:
- Increase expansion revenue from retained customers, reducing the absolute impact of churn on growth
- Change the product category to one with higher inherent stickiness (data lock-in, workflow integration, network effects)
- Redefine the customer segment toward those who have lower historical churn rates for your type of product
- Build switching costs into the product architecture (data export friction, integrations that are hard to rebuild elsewhere)

---

**Most important observation:**
The exit survey approach operates at Level 1 only. It assumes that what churning customers say they wanted is both true and addressable. The Level 2 analysis shows there are five other concepts to serve the same goal — several of which may be higher-leverage than adding features. The Level 3 analysis reveals that some of the most durable solutions involve changing what the product *is*, not what it *has*.
