# creativity-lateral-thinking

> Escapes the dominant idea — the direction you keep returning to — and generates genuinely different directions.

**Trigger:** `/s4h-creativity-lateral-thinking`

---

## What it does

Names the dominant idea explicitly, sets it aside, and generates directions that don't involve it. Produces 5+ genuinely different directions, not variations on the same theme. Based on Edward de Bono's lateral thinking methodology.

---

## When to use

- "I keep thinking about this the same way"
- Stuck on a problem — every approach feels like a variation of the last one
- Want to find the direction you haven't considered
- Need to escape the frame before doing any other creative work

## When *not* to use

- You don't have a dominant idea yet → [water-logic](water-logic.md) to explore first
- You need to evaluate ideas, not generate them → [plus-minus-interesting](plus-minus-interesting.md)
- The problem framing itself might be wrong → [assumption-excavator](assumption-excavator.md) first

---

## Example

**Input:**
> We're trying to increase engagement in our community product. We've been thinking about this in terms of better notifications, better content feeds, and better onboarding. None of it is working.

**Output:**

**Dominant idea:** Make the product better at pushing relevant content and pulling users back in.

*Setting this aside for the rest of this session.*

**Directions that don't involve improving push/pull mechanics:**

1. **Make leaving the community mean something.** What if the engagement problem is that there are no stakes to disengagement? What if leaving a conversation cost you something — reputation, access, a thread you care about?

2. **Remove the audience.** Most community products assume engagement = posting publicly. What if the friction is the audience size? 1:1 conversations in a community context have consistently higher engagement than broadcast.

3. **Give members a job.** Engaged communities have members with roles — moderators, experts, welcomers. Not job titles — actual functions that require showing up. What's the minimum viable role structure?

4. **Find the 10% who are deeply engaged and build for them.** Most engagement optimization tries to move the median. What if you built features only the top decile would love, and let them pull others in?

5. **Make contribution asymmetric.** The cost of contributing (time, exposure) often exceeds the benefit (responses, recognition). What if contributing created durable value — a resource that lives on — rather than ephemeral value that disappears in the feed?

6. **Remove features.** What's in the product right now that's competing for attention without adding to community? Sometimes engagement goes up when there's less to do, not more.

**Most promising direction to develop:**
Direction 3 — roles and functions — because it's the only one that creates intrinsic motivation for showing up, rather than extrinsic pull. Every deeply engaged community has members who feel responsible for it. That responsibility doesn't emerge from better notifications.
