# creativity-water-logic

> Explores without judgment — follows where ideas lead rather than testing whether they're right.

**Trigger:** `/creativity-water-logic`

---

## What it does

Applies de Bono's water logic: instead of asking "is this true?" (rock logic), asks "where does this lead?" Follows the natural flow of ideas without evaluation, mapping territory before deciding what matters. Produces a map of connections, directions, and possibilities that judgment-first thinking misses.

---

## When to use

- Early-stage exploration where premature evaluation is killing promising directions
- "Follow this thread" — without yet knowing where it goes
- Open-ended territory where "is this right?" is the wrong question
- Need to map the landscape before picking a path
- Creative work where evaluation is being applied too early

## When *not* to use

- You need to evaluate a specific idea → [plus-minus-interesting](plus-minus-interesting.md)
- You need to generate options against a specific goal → [alternatives](alternatives.md)
- You need structure and convergence → [six-hats](six-hats.md)

---

## Example

**Input:**
> I'm thinking about building something at the intersection of AI and mental health. I don't have a clear idea yet. Let me explore.

**Output:**

*Following the flow — no evaluation until the map is complete.*

**AI and mental health → what does that lead to?**

It leads to: the gap between when someone needs support and when professional support is available. Which leads to: 3am, the weekend, the moment right after a hard conversation. Which leads to: what kind of support is possible in that gap? Not therapy — something else. What kind of something else? Presence. The feeling of being heard without judgment. Which leads to: is AI actually good at that, or just patient? It's patient. Patience might be enough for some people in some moments.

**That thread → what else does it connect to?**

The chronic vs. acute distinction. Mental health support is designed for chronic conditions but most people encounter acute moments. A crisis line is for acute. Therapy is for chronic. The gap in between — the hard week, the difficult decision, the low-grade spiral — has almost nothing designed for it. Which leads to: is that gap an access problem or a product problem? Both. It's access: therapy is expensive and has waitlists. It's product: there's nothing designed for that specific texture of need.

**AI specifically → what does it change?**

The limiting factor in the "someone who listens" model has always been cost and availability. AI removes the availability constraint almost completely. Which leads to: if availability is solved, what's the remaining problem? Judgment. Is an AI saying "that sounds hard" actually helpful, or is it a pale substitute that might even prevent people from getting real help? Which leads to: the risk isn't that AI mental health is harmful, it's that it's adequate enough to delay better treatment.

**Where the flow leads:**

The most interesting space is narrow but real: the gap between "I'm fine" and "I need professional help" — where someone knows something is off but doesn't have language for it yet, or doesn't know if it's serious enough to warrant action. That gap has no good tools. An AI that helps people name what they're experiencing — not treat it, just name it — might be genuinely useful and lower-risk than a tool that tries to provide support.

**One direction worth developing:**
A tool that helps people understand and articulate what they're feeling, connects them to appropriate resources, and explicitly refers out rather than competing with therapy. The job: "help me figure out what's going on with me" rather than "make me feel better."
